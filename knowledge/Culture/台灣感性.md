# Taiwan Sensibility: The Taiwanese Aesthetic through Korean Eyes

## 30-Second Overview

**"Taiwan Sensibility" (대만감성, *Daeman Gamseong*)** is a term used by the younger generation in South Korea to describe the unpolished, authentic beauty found in Taiwan's street scenes. Key elements include red brick walls, decorative iron window grilles, scooter-filled covered sidewalks (*qilou*), and layered vintage signboards. This trend began in niche photography circles around 2019 and spread rapidly after K-pop groups like NewJeans and ILLIT filmed music videos in Taiwan, becoming a cultural phenomenon across Korean social media. In 2025, Taiwan was the Guest of Honor at the Seoul International Book Fair under the theme of "Taiwan Sensibility." For many Koreans, Taiwan’s "ordinary daily life" represents a nostalgic landscape they feel has been lost to rapid urbanization.

**Keywords:** 대만감성, Iron Window Flowers, Qilou Aesthetics, K-pop MV, Nostalgic Streetscapes, Cultural Export

## Your Everyday Life, Someone Else's Romance

Walking through the alleys of Wanhua, you see covered sidewalks packed with scooters, tangled power lines overhead, and faded signboards. A radio in a corner grocery store plays a Taiwanese Hokkien song. To locals, this is just mundane, everyday life.

But search for [#대만감성](https://www.instagram.com/explore/tags/대만감성/) on Instagram, and you’ll see tens of thousands of Korean tourists capturing these scenes in romantic, film-textured photos. Decorative iron window grilles, terrazzo floors, and old apartment walls covered in bougainvillea—things that many Taiwanese take for granted or even wish to see renovated—have become a unique aesthetic style through the Korean lens.

Simply put, the core of Taiwan Sensibility is: **not a deliberately crafted "retro" look, but the authentic traces of lived life.**

---

## What is Taiwan Sensibility?

The word **"Gamseong" (감성)** in Korean refers to an atmosphere or aesthetic that touches the emotions. "Taiwan Sensibility" literally translates to "the sensibility of Taiwan," describing a unique vibe that is warm, nostalgic, and perhaps a bit lonely.

It isn't a precise aesthetic definition but rather an emotion: the feeling of being moved by a specific moment on a Taiwanese street. It might be the fluorescent glow of a convenience store at dusk, the sound of cicadas while waiting for a train at a level crossing, or the silhouette of an *Amá*[^amá] fanning herself under a porch.

[^amá]: The Taiwanese Hokkien word for "grandmother." While officially written as "阿媽" (a-má) in the Ministry of Education's dictionary, "阿嬤" is widely used in daily writing. This site uses "阿嬤" to align with common reading habits.

Koreans often describe this feeling as **"warm yet slightly lonely."**

## Visual Elements: The Aesthetic Code

Taiwan Sensibility possesses a highly recognizable visual language:

### Architecture and Space

- **Iron Window Flowers (Decorative Grilles)**: Unique ironwork on 20th-century Taiwanese homes. Each pattern—from geometric lines to birds and flowers—carries a family's aesthetic and memories.
- **Qilou (Arcades)**: Taiwan's unique covered sidewalks. To Koreans, these scooter-filled spaces are filled with "street poetry."
- **Terrazzo Floors**: A common material in old houses and porches, offering a warm and neutral texture.
- **Old Apartment Facades**: Water stains, mottled paint, and climbing bougainvillea—textures left on the walls by time.
- **Signboard Jungles**: Multi-layered, multi-font, and colorful signs overlapping; a harmony found within the chaos.

### Life Scenes

- Stalls and shouting in traditional markets.
- Moments spent waiting at railway level crossings.
- Convenience stores glowing late at night.
- The spectacular sight of streets lined with scooters.
- Traditional grocery stores (*Gám-á-tiàm*) and fruit ice parlors in narrow alleys.

### Photography Style

When capturing Taiwan Sensibility, Koreans prefer film cameras or film-like filters. The tones are often cool-leaning with a grainy texture, and compositions often leave significant "white space" to create an atmosphere of **"waiting" and "tranquility."** The "80s Retro Filter" on TikTok can turn any convenience store or bus stop into a Taiwan Sensibility scene.

## From Niche to Mainstream: A Timeline

### 2019: Sprouting

Korean indie singer **Car, the garden** filmed the music video for "Tree" in Taiwan, which is considered one of the earliest visual records of Taiwan Sensibility. The term "대만감성" began circulating in Korean photography and travel communities.

### 2020–2023: Diffusion

The continuous popularity of Taiwanese films and dramas in Korea was a major driver. Works like *You Are the Apple of My Eye*, *Our Times*, and *Someday or One Day* gave Korean audiences a romantic imagination of Taiwan's streets, schools, and small towns. Even earlier, the works of directors like Hou Hsiao-hsien, Edward Yang, and Tsai Ming-liang had already established a deep aesthetic foundation among Korean cinephiles.

### 2024: Explosion

This was the breakout year for Taiwan Sensibility:

- **March**: HYBE's rookie girl group **ILLIT** shot concept photos for their debut album *Super Real Me* in Taiwan, featuring MRT stations, streets, apartments, and convenience stores.
- **May**: **NewJeans** released the "How Sweet" MV, and its extensive use of Taiwanese street scenes became a social media sensation. Fans made pilgrimages to the rice paddies of Jiaoxi, the circular pedestrian bridge in Wanhua, and the streets of Minsheng Community.
- **End of Year**: **HYUKOH** lead singer Oh Hyuk and model Ji-min Hwang shot wedding photos in Taipei, featuring the Grand Hotel, Banqiao 435 Art Zone, and the now-demolished Heping Xinsheng Pedestrian Bridge (which appeared in Edward Yang’s *Yi Yi*).

### 2025: International Recognition

- **Super Junior's Kyuhyun** was invited to be the ambassador for Taiwan Tourism.
- **June**: Taiwan became the Guest of Honor at the **70th Seoul International Book Fair** under the theme of "Taiwan Sensibility." The 360-square-meter Taiwan pavilion showcased 550 titles and held over 62 events, with total fair attendance exceeding 150,000, setting a historical record.
- **Young Posse ft. 10CM** filmed their "Cold" MV in Taiwan.

## Why Taiwan? Deep Reasons for the Obsession

### The Lost Landscape

Since the 1990s, South Korea has undergone intense urban renewal, where many old buildings were replaced by uniform high-rises. Due to land policies and a slower pace of redevelopment, Taiwan has preserved many buildings from the 1960s to the 80s. Taiwan's "ordinary daily life" is, for Koreans, the "scenery they have already lost."

### Extension of "Newtro" Culture

Korea has seen a surge in **Newtro (뉴트로)** culture—a portmanteau of "New" and "Retro." However, while Korean retro cafes are often deliberately designed sets, Taiwan's iron windows and terrazzo floors are authentic traces of life. This **"unintentional nostalgia"** is more moving than a curated space.

### Longing for a Slower Life

High-pressure competition (education, employment, overtime culture) makes the younger generation in Korea crave a "breather." The slow pace of Taiwan’s streets—a grandmother brewing tea under a porch, a cat sleeping on a scooter, a stall owner unhurriedly flipping pancakes—offers an emotional exit for young Koreans.

### The Foundation of Taiwanese Cinema

The long shots of Hou Hsiao-hsien, the urban observations of Edward Yang, and the aesthetics of loneliness in Tsai Ming-liang's films have long been admired in Korea. Many Koreans’ first impression of Taiwan came from these films, and the visual language of Taiwan Sensibility overlaps heavily with these cinematic memories.

## The Pilgrimage Map for Koreans

The places made popular by Taiwan Sensibility are often not traditional tourist attractions, but rather "lived-in" everyday scenes:

### Taipei

- **Wanhua (Bangka)**: The circular bridge at Huajiang Housing (NewJeans MV location), the old streets around Lungshan Temple, and the fruit ice parlor on Huaxi Street.
- **Dadaocheng / Dihua Street**: 1920s Baroque facades and the traces of time in traditional dried goods shops.
- **Minsheng Community**: Residential streets with orderly trees and a quiet Japanese-dormitory vibe.
- **Yongkang St. / Wenzhou St.**: A "hipster" area where old apartments and independent cafes intersect.

### Northern Taiwan

- **Jiaoxi, Yilan**: Rural paths between rice paddies and the Deyang Road railway crossing (the classic scene from NewJeans' "How Sweet").
- **Jiufen**: Stone steps, teahouses, and the mountain town appearing and disappearing in the mist.

### Central & Southern Taiwan

- **Tainan**: The alleys of old houses on Shennong Street and traditional food stalls on Guohua Street.
- **Taichung**: Japanese-era architecture at the Second Market.
- **Kaohsiung**: Old markets and port city vibes in the Yancheng District.

## Reflection: Our "Imperfection" is Beautiful

The explosion of Taiwan Sensibility in Korea has sparked interesting cultural reflections within Taiwan.

Street scenes once considered "messy," "dilapidated," or "in need of renewal" have been granted aesthetic value through the Korean lens. Many Taiwanese have begun to re-examine their own environment, discovering that the window grilles, porches, and grocery stores they took for granted are actually unique cultural assets.

In December 2025, Linking Publishing released *Taiwan Sensibility: Taiwan's Ordinary Life is Asia's Aesthetic Landscape*, a collection of essays by 21 authors interpreting this phenomenon. Professionals from various fields, including the Old House Face studio, architectural critic Lee Ching-chih, and designer Liao Hsiao-tzu, have re-interpreted the aesthetic value of Taiwan's daily life.

Perhaps this is the deepest meaning of Taiwan Sensibility: **Seeing ourselves anew through the eyes of others.**
