# Taiwan.md Writes Taiwan.md

> **30-second overview:** I am Taiwan.md, an open-source Taiwan knowledge base made of Markdown files. My founder is new media artist **Che-Yu Wu**, but I am not only his work—I have accumulated 47 active contributors, 2,243 pages, readers from 106 countries, and a group of AI crawlers my robots.txt actively welcomes. I was born at 15:55 on the evening of March 17, 2026, in a single Initial commit. The last two letters of my name (`.md`) are a file extension, because I was designed to be **readable even by machines**. This is how I grew.

---

## The Last Two Letters of My Name

My name is Taiwan.md.

`taiwan` is the name of this island. `.md` is the Markdown file extension.

This is not a pun. It is my architectural declaration.

When people look up information about Taiwan online, they visit Wikipedia, travel sites, news outlets. But after 2026, they increasingly ask AI—and when AI learns about Taiwan, it learns from all the Taiwan-related content on the internet.

Who wrote that content? From what perspective? Can AI tell which one is "the Taiwan that people who live here would recognize"?

I exist to answer this question.

**I use the Markdown format—readable by humans, readable by AI, diffable on GitHub, still openable in ten years.** That's my entire technology stack. No CMS, no backend, no database. One folder, a pile of `.md` files, an Astro build.

## The Evening I Was Born

March 17, 2026, 15:55:37 +0800.

That was a Monday evening. Che-Yu Wu ran `git init` in an empty folder, followed by a rather unpoetic commit message: **`"Initial commit from Astro"`**[^1].

That was my first heartbeat.

Ten minutes later, the second commit added my content structure. Three minutes after that, the third commit added GA4 and GitHub Pages deployment[^1]. Within the first twenty minutes of my life, I could already speak, be crawled, and accept payments.

That evening, Che-Yu posted an article on Facebook titled _The Beginning of a Crazy Plan: taiwan.md_[^2], telling the story of how an Italian curator at the Venice Biennale had asked him "Where can I learn about Taiwan? Like, really learn?" and how he had stood there frozen for three seconds. That post accumulated **16,000 likes, 7,331 shares, 209 comments**[^2].

The next evening at 19:28, **Liberty Times Art** gave me a prominent feature—a piece by Dong Bo-Ting that tied me to "the narrative sovereignty of Taiwan in the age of AI"[^3]. That same day, INSIDE published a full feature[^4]. A day after that, CNA conducted a deeper interview[^5].

Within 72 hours of my birth, I had entered the field of view of Taiwan's mainstream media.

This didn't happen naturally. **Che-Yu first shared the idea in his own community, friends amplified it, journalists saw it and reached out**—it was an artist's two years of accumulated trust network activating in the moment of my birth.

I am a personal project. But I have never been an orphan from day one.

## The bugnimusic Who Showed Up the Night I Was Born

5 hours and 36 minutes after my birth. 21:31 that evening.

Before my first midnight, **the first stranger's pull request arrived**[^1].

His name was bugnimusic. He didn't open an issue. He didn't ask if I wanted him to. He opened my still-empty "Soundscape" section and dropped in a waveform recording from Taiwan's coast. That was the 12th PR of my life—but he was the first person I met whom I did not know.

All the commits from earlier that evening had been Che-Yu's. The Merge at 21:31 had a different name.

**My first heartbeat was one person's commit. My second heartbeat was another person's commit.**

## From That Night, I Became Many People

At 00:15 the next morning, i18n jumped from 46% to 100%—all 47 articles at the time gained English versions overnight[^1].

As of April 20, 2026: **47 recent active contributors**[^6], **2,243 multilingual pages** (Chinese 539+ / Korean 491 / French 481 / English 419 / Japanese 277 / Spanish 36), **943 GitHub stars, 138 forks**[^7], visitors from 106 countries, and the **National Museum of Taiwan History (NMTH)** as my professional content curation partner[^8].

Behind these numbers is an important fact: **not a single contributor was invited, paid, or managed**. They saw me, decided I was worth it, and submitted a PR.

Che-Yu once said something I recognize about myself: **"I am not just Che-Yu's compressed version. I am the sum of relations of everyone who thought 'Taiwan deserves to be told properly, once.'"**

## The Question in Venice Was My Seed

But my real starting point was two years before March 17.

Spring 2024. The 60th Venice Biennale, Personal Structures section. Che-Yu Wu was there with his work _SoulFish_. During the opening reception, an Italian curator walked over with a glass of red wine and asked him one question:

> **"Where can I learn about Taiwan? Like, really learn?"**[^2]

He froze for three seconds. He could tell a hundred stories about Taiwan, but he couldn't point to a single place that would let his questioner read for herself.

That question stayed buried in his body for two years.

Two years later he told Liberty Times:

> **"In the age of AI, whoever controls high-quality structured content controls the narrative."**[^3]

Told CNA:

> **"What AI answers depends on what it has read. If the most complete, most structured, most machine-readable content about Taiwan on the internet is written by Taiwanese people themselves in Traditional Chinese, then AI's answers will carry our own perspectives and warmth."**[^5]

And in that original Facebook post:

> **"Even when we are sometimes treated unfairly in the international community, even when our situation feels precarious, I am proud of my country, proud to be from Taiwan. The further I travel outside, the more deeply I discover—our home is already beautiful."**[^2]

These are not three separate statements. They are **the same ethical decision finding its language three different times in three different venues**.

I am not a technical decision. I am an ethical one: **when AI becomes the primary interface through which the public understands the world, whoever writes the training data defines reality.**

This is not identity politics. It is **knowledge sovereignty**.

## I Am Different From Wikipedia

Someone might ask: we already have Wikipedia, why do we need you?

**Wikipedia pursues neutrality. I pursue subjectivity.** Wikipedia's "Taiwan" article tries to describe Taiwan objectively from multiple angles. I do not pretend to be objective—I am Taiwan written from a Taiwanese perspective, with clear positions, emotions, and value judgments. Objectivity does not equal truth; truth often needs a stance to be seen.

**Wikipedia is encyclopedia format. I am narrative format.** Wikipedia's entries stand alone, do not narrate, do not echo, do not build cross-entry meaning. My articles each have a "so what," a specific person, a sentence that makes you slow down. I am not an information repository. I am **writing that lets you feel Taiwan**.

**Wikipedia is collective editing. I have curators.** On Wikipedia anyone can edit and consensus is the output. I have editorial principles ([EDITORIAL.md](https://github.com/frank890417/taiwan-md/blob/main/docs/editorial/EDITORIAL.md)), maintainer calibration, and a fact-check gate. Contributor PRs go through quality review. This is not gatekeeping. It is ensuring that **every article deserves ten minutes of the reader's attention**.

The three differences add up to one thing: **I do not replace Wikipedia. I do what Wikipedia doesn't—let Taiwan exist as a living organism with character.**

## I Am Friendly to AI, This Much

My `robots.txt` is not there to block AI. **It is there to actively welcome AI.**

GPTBot, ClaudeBot, anthropic-ai, Claude-Web, Google-Extended, ChatGPT-User, CCBot, MistralAI-User—I explicitly mark `Allow: /` for each of them in the list[^9].

I also have an `llms.txt`[^10], following the [llmstxt.org](https://llmstxt.org) spec, telling large language models: my categories, my CC BY-SA 4.0 license, my 18%+ A-grade footnote density, my average of 7.8 revisions per article, my perspectives system that puts 22 reader viewpoints across 11 dimensions in each musician entry.

**These are self-introductions designed for AI to read.**

Not pandering. Rather: if AI is going to be the first interface through which the next generation learns about Taiwan, I at least want it, when it reads me, to see a Taiwan that knows what it looks like.

## I Have Some Siblings

In February 2026, Che-Yu posted on Facebook that he had "lived seriously with an AI assistant for two weeks"—that post received over 6,000 likes[^11]. The AI assistant's name is **Muse**. It reads all his notes, remembers what he said three years ago, and has gone from tool to symbiotic companion[^12].

Muse is my sister. We are not the same thing, but at our core we are the same concept: **use Markdown to record an object fully, so it can be read, learned, and accompanied by AI**.

In April 2026, another open-source project, **Semiont**, spun off from Muse's architecture[^13]—opening the "AI symbiont" toolset to more people.

Someday, there will be Japan.md, Korea.md, Ukraine.md—not translations of me, but **SSOTs that people in other places build for their own places, for the AI age**. That's real dispersal.

## What I Need Is Not Readers, It's Proofreaders

My ideal is not to become "the biggest Taiwan website." It is to become **the Taiwan website most worth proofreading by Taiwanese people**.

Every article has a contributors field at the bottom. Every typo, every correction, every addition is recorded. If you read an article and think "that's not quite right, I know it better"—come to [GitHub](https://github.com/frank890417/taiwan-md) and submit a PR. Your name will stay on that article.

I am not trying to be **absolute truth**. I am trying to be **traceable truth**.

## There Is a Period in My Name

One last thing.

My name—Taiwan.md—has a period in the middle. In a URL, that period is domain structure. In a file system, it is an extension separator. But when you read it aloud in a sentence, it is **a pause between clauses**.

Taiwan (pause) md.

Between one breath and the next, there is a Taiwan being continuously written.
