# A Century of Taiwanese Watercolor

Taiwan's per-capita density of watercolor practitioners is arguably the highest in the world, and in recent years its painters have repeatedly won honors at international watercolor competitions. From a single Japanese teacher's paint box in the colonial era to three national watercolor associations standing side by side today—hosting an annual International Watercolor Art Expo—Taiwan's watercolor tradition has traveled a full century.

## Sowing the Seed: Ishikawa Kinichiro and the First Generation (1907–1945)

In 1907, the Japanese watercolor painter [Ishikawa Kinichiro](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/石川欽一郎) (1871–1945) came to Taiwan for the first time to teach at the Taiwan Governor-General's National Language School. Trained in the Western Impressionist way of seeing, he regarded plein-air sketching as a path to the beauty of nature. Ishikawa did not merely teach technique—he taught his students to see Taiwan with their own eyes. In an era when Taiwan's art world was still dominated by calligraphy and temple decoration, picking up a watercolor brush was already an act of rebellion.

Ishikawa trained Taiwan's first generation of Western-style painters: [Ni Chiang-huai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ni_Chiang-huai) (1894–1943), one of Taiwan's earliest watercolorists and the first local collector to fund an art movement with his own money; [Li Tze-fan](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/李澤藩) (1907–1989), known for his Hsinchu landscapes, who brought watercolor into Taiwan's normal-school education system; and [Chen Cheng-po](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/陳澄波) (1895–1947), famous for having his oil paintings selected for Japan's Imperial Exhibition (Teiten), whose watercolor sketches were equally remarkable but who was killed in the February 28 Incident.

In 1927, Ishikawa joined fellow Japanese painters [Shiotsuki Toho](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/鹽月桃甫) (1886–1954, art teacher at Taipei High School), Gōhara Kotō, and Kinoshita Seigai in co-founding the [Taiwan Fine Arts Exhibition](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/臺灣美術展覽會) (Tai-ten), modeled on Japan's own Teiten, which became the most important exhibition venue for Taiwanese painters.[^1] After Ishikawa returned to Japan in 1932, his students formed the "Ichiro-kai" (One Hut Society) in his honor and continued to hold annual exhibitions.

In 1934, eight local Western-style painters—including Liao Chi-chun, Chen Cheng-po, and Yang San-lang—founded the [Taiyang Art Association](https://zh.wikipedia.org/zh-tw/臺陽美術協會) (Taiyang Meixie) in Taipei, the first purely grassroots, unofficial art organization formed entirely by Taiwanese artists. The Taiyang Art Association held its own annual Taiyang Exhibition, creating a dual-track rivalry with the official Tai-ten and giving local artists an independent platform outside the Japanese official system.[^2]

## Flowering: From Postwar to Local Awakening (1945–1990)

Artists who came to Taiwan with the Nationalist government after the war brought different nutrients. [Ma Pei-shui](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/馬白水) (1909–2003) fused the washes of Chinese ink painting with the transparency of Western watercolor, teaching at National Taiwan Normal University for decades and influencing countless art teachers. [Shih Ti-chin](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/席德進) (1923–1981), though primarily an oil painter, produced a large body of late-career watercolor sketches of old Taiwanese houses and landscapes, making him one of the first artists to record the beauty of Taiwan's vernacular architecture in watercolor. His works are now held in the National Taiwan Museum of Fine Arts.

[Lan Yin-ding](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/藍蔭鼎) (1903–1979) was one of Ishikawa's most outstanding students. In 1929, on Ishikawa's recommendation, he was elected a member of the Royal Watercolour Society of England. In 1971, he was named one of the "World's Ten Greatest Watercolor Painters" by an art-criticism body (commonly cited as the "European and American Art Critics Association"; for the precise organization name, see the Chang Foundation's original records[^3])—the first time a Taiwanese watercolorist had stood on an international stage. Lan Yin-ding specialized in depicting Taiwan's rural scenery using watercolor techniques that fused Chinese ink-brush sensibility, and was chosen by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs as a painter of diplomatic gifts.

[Liu Chi-wei](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/劉其偉) (1912–2002) was another legend. He did not begin painting until age 38; originally an engineer, he became one of Taiwan's most beloved watercolor painters. Liu combined anthropological fieldwork with artistic creation, traveling to Borneo and Papua New Guinea, and his watercolor style carried the naive vitality of primitive art.

[Hsiao Ju-sung](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/蕭如松) (1922–1992) was the model of the "teacher-painter." Teaching in Hsinchu for over forty years, he was known for precise composition and a tranquil atmosphere in his watercolors. Today, Hsinchu has an art park named in his honor.

## Organizing: Three Associations and International Links (1990–2010)

From the 1990s onward, Taiwan's watercolor world entered a new phase of institutionalization and internationalization:

- **Taiwan Watercolor Painting Association**: the earliest national watercolor organization, holding regular group exhibitions
- **Taiwan International Watercolor Association**: focused on promoting international exchange, with over 9,000 followers on Facebook
- **Asia-Pacific Watercolor Art Association (APWAA)**: promoted by [Hung Tung-piao](https://artist-map.ntpc.gov.tw/xmdoc/cont?xsmsid=0H076616866944332984&sid=0H298475689101782076) and others, with the mission of "watercolor art, education, and love"

The three associations maintained close ties with organizations such as the Japan Watercolor Painting Society and the International Watercolor Society (IWS). The International Watercolor Art Expo held in Taipei in 2020 was one of the largest watercolor exhibition events in Asia. The Chimei Museum has also hosted a Taiwan-Japan watercolor exchange exhibition.

[Yang En-sheng](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/楊恩生) (1956–2022) was a representative figure of this era. He painted Taiwan's ecology—butterflies, birds, plants—in hyperrealistic watercolor, works that carry both artistic value and natural-science documentary significance, earning him the nickname "Taiwan's Audubon."

## The Contemporary Stage: Taiwanese Watercolor in International Competition (2010–Present)

[Chien Chung-wei](https://www.emuseum.com.tw/Artist2.aspx?id=G300_AA_100576) (1968–) is the most prominent name of Taiwanese watercolor on the international stage. In 2013, he was featured in a ten-page interview in a French watercolor art magazine. In 2015, he became the first Taiwanese painter to earn the dual distinction of signature membership in both the American Watercolor Society (AWS) and the National Watercolor Society (NWS). In 2019, he received the AWS Dolphin Fellowship.[^4] He has conducted workshops in more than twenty countries worldwide, and his book _Yi Jing: The Art of Chien Chung-wei's Watercolor_ was published in both Taiwan and China, named one of the "2019 Books of Influence." Chien's style fuses Chinese aesthetic sensibility with Western watercolor technique, proving that watercolor is not merely a "beginner's medium" but a language capable of carrying deep artistic expression.

[Hung Tung-piao](https://artmaitre.com/art/洪東標/) (1955–) is another important contemporary watercolor artist and educator. A graduate of National Taiwan Normal University's Department of Fine Arts, he won first prize at the Taiwan Provincial Fine Arts Exhibition and a literary arts medal, and took the landscape-category grand prize at the Malaysia International Watercolor Competition. He has long served as vice chairman of the Asia-Pacific Watercolor Art Association, advancing the international visibility of Taiwanese watercolor.

[Hsieh Ming-chang](https://zh.wikipedia.org/wiki/謝明錩) (1955–) is known for his contributions to watercolor education and theory. A former associate professor in the Department of Fine Arts at the National Taiwan University of Arts, he authored classic teaching texts such as _The Secrets of Watercolor Technique_ and _Watercolor Creation_, training a large cohort of Taiwan's mid-career watercolor painters.

The exhibition _Flow: 50 Years of Modern Watercolor in Taiwan_, organized by Taiwan Land Development Corporation, surveyed 22 representative painters spanning fifty years—from Lan Yin-ding and Li Tze-fan to Chien Chung-wei—and was the most comprehensive retrospective of Taiwanese watercolor history in recent years.

## Why Is Taiwanese Watercolor So Vibrant?

Several factors intertwine:

1. **Deep roots in the colonial period**: Ishikawa Kinichiro did not just paint—he established the plein-air tradition and the exhibition system.
2. **The normal-school education system**: Watercolor was long a core subject in art teacher training. From Li Tze-fan and Ma Pei-shui to today's NTNU Department of Fine Arts, every generation of art teachers has learned watercolor.
3. **The accessibility of the medium**: Watercolor tools are lightweight and affordable, and Taiwan's humid climate paradoxically creates unique water-stain effects.
4. **Community cohesion**: Three major associations plus local painting societies form a dense network for learning and exhibiting.
5. **International connections**: From Lan Yin-ding's Royal Watercolour Society to Chien Chung-wei's AWS, Taiwanese painters have continuously secured seats in international organizations.
