> **30-Second Overview:** In 1962, the Taiwan Stock Exchange opened with only eighteen listed companies. Sixty-four years later, in early 2026, the weighted index surpassed 30,000 points, with more than 1,050 listed companies and a registered account count surpassing 13 million. Along the way, the market weathered the collective trauma of the 1990 ten-thousand-point bubble, the collapse of trust caused by the Hung Yuan Institution's 94-billion-NT-dollar fraud, a structural shift from retail-dominant to institutional trading, and the era of universal investing reshaped jointly by ETFs and TSMC (台積電 — zh only). The Taiwan stock market is not just a number — it is the real-time electrocardiogram of Taiwan's economic confidence.

On February 12, 1990, Taiwan's weighted stock price index surged to 12,682 points — an all-time high. Someone set off firecrackers outside the stock exchange. At the time, 4.6 million active trading accounts were open across Taiwan, nearly every family had someone buying stocks, and 90% of trading volume came from retail investors.[^1] Taxi drivers discussed hot tips, aunties in the market placed orders by phone from brokerage offices, and civil servants secretly tracked the ticker tape at their desks.

Eight months later, the index had fallen to 2,485 points. Down 80%.

Nobody set off firecrackers.

## Eighteen Companies at the Starting Line

The Taiwan Stock Exchange was founded in October 1961 and formally opened for trading on February 9, 1962. First chairman was Koo Chen-fu; the first batch of listed companies included eighteen: Taiwan Cement, Taiwan Paper, Taiwan Agriculture and Forestry, Taiwan Industrial and Mining, and others.[^2] The Taiwan stock market of that era resembled a small-town general store: few goods, few visitors, so little trading you could hear the sound of pages turning.

The transformation began in the mid-1980s. Taiwan's economic miracle had accumulated enormous private wealth, and the NT dollar had appreciated against the US dollar from 40 to 25 — exporters suddenly found themselves holding 60% more cash. That money needed somewhere to go. In 1986, after thirty-four years, the index finally hit 1,000 points. Then in fewer than three years it blew past 10,000 points in June 1989.[^3]

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> "Taiwan's money floods up to ankle level" (台灣錢淹腳目) — this phrase was born in that era. Too much money, too few places to put it; the stock market and real estate became enormous reservoirs. The problem was, these reservoirs had no flood gates.

## The Bubble, Hung Yuan, and a Generation's Trauma

The 1990 crash was not just falling numbers. It destroyed an entire generation of Taiwanese trust in financial markets.

From 12,682 to 2,485, countless people bought more as prices fell — "catching the bottom" three times, five times, ten times, each time convinced it had finally bottomed. Many families gave back everything they had earned in the bull market and then some — mortgaging their houses, losing their retirement savings.[^4] Hot money from abroad had already retreated; those left holding the knife were all domestic retail investors.

Making things worse was the Hung Yuan Institution. This underground investment firm had been absorbing private capital since 1981 at a monthly interest rate of 4% (monthly return of 4%). At its peak, it had absorbed 94 billion NT dollars in deposits. On January 9, 1990, Hung Yuan announced the suspension of withdrawals, leaving more than 160,000 creditors suddenly wiped out.[^5] The victims were predominantly civil servants, teachers, and military personnel who had put their life savings and retirement funds in, because 4% monthly returns looked "guaranteed safe."

> **⚠️ Contested Territory**
> The mastermind of the Hung Yuan case, Shen Chang-sheng, was sentenced to only seven years and paroled in 1994. Of the 94 billion NT dollars absorbed, only about 4 billion was eventually recovered. "Seven years in prison for 160,000 people's retirement savings" became the most painful scar in Taiwan's financial regulatory history.

The aftermath of this double blow lasted a decade. Throughout the 1990s, "stocks" were a dirty word in many Taiwanese families. Parents warned children: don't touch stocks, that's gambling.

## The Institutional Era: From Casino to Market

In the 2000s, the constitution of the Taiwan stock market began to change.

Foreign institutional ownership steadily climbed from below 5% in the early 1990s. The government relaxed restrictions on foreign investment and introduced the Qualified Foreign Institutional Investor (QFII) system, channeling international capital into the market. Institutional investors replaced retail investors as the dominant force; the trading logic shifted from "following hot tips" to "reading financial reports."[^6]

In June 2003, Yuanta Investment Trust issued Taiwan's first ETF: Yuanta Taiwan 50 (0050). This index fund tracking the top fifty listed companies in Taiwan was met with indifference when it launched. Nobody could have predicted that twenty years later it would become the "national ETF" with assets approaching 1.4 trillion NT dollars and more than 2.74 million beneficiaries.[^7]

0050 accomplished one thing: it allowed people who didn't understand individual stocks to participate in Taiwan's economic growth. You don't need to know what nanometer process node TSMC is at — you only need to believe that Taiwan's top fifty companies will collectively move upward over time. This was the starting point of passive investing in Taiwan.

## TSMC's Gravitational Field

Talking about Taiwan's stock market without mentioning TSMC (台積電 — zh only) is like talking about the solar system without mentioning the sun.

In March 2026, TSMC accounted for approximately 45% of the weighted index weight. A one-NT-dollar movement in TSMC's share price moves the index by approximately 8 points.[^8] One company accounting for nearly half the weight of an entire market is extremely rare across all major global stock exchanges.

This is both Taiwan's strength and its vulnerability. TSMC does well, Taiwan's market does well. TSMC sneezes, Taiwan's market catches a bad cold. After the AI demand explosion from 2024 onward, TSMC's revenues hit record after record, quarterly EPS reaching a historic high of 19.5 NT dollars, driving Taiwan's index up from 20,000 to 30,000 points.[^9]

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Taiwan's greatest geopolitical risk and its greatest economic pillar are the same thing: semiconductors. In some sense, the fate of Taiwan's stock market is the fate of TSMC. Investors have grown accustomed to this fact — but familiarity does not mean safety.

## An Island of 13 Million Accounts

By the end of 2024, Taiwan's cumulative stock account registrations reached 13.21 million, with more than 700,000 new accounts opened in that single year alone. On an island with a total population of 23.4 million, more than half the population holds a securities account.[^10]

More noteworthy is the structural shift: in 2024, new account openings by those aged under 30 accounted for 67% of total new openings, and investors between 20 and 30 exceeded 1.72 million. The younger generation no longer treats stocks as gambling, but as a financial management tool. Odd-lot trading, regular fixed-amount investing, and ETF subscription have become their routine.

From the 1990s' "stocks are gambling" to the 2020s' "not investing is the real risk" — it took thirty years for Taiwanese society's attitude toward capital markets to turn around.

| ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| 4.6 million active accounts                     | 13.21 million registered accounts                 |
| Retail investors = 90% of trading volume        | Institutional investors and ETFs steadily growing |

## After 30,000

In January 2026, Taiwan's stock market cleared 30,000 points. Nobody set off firecrackers.

Not because no one was excited, but because the market had matured. The ten-thousand-point high thirty years ago was a bubble inflated by retail investors trading in and out; the 30,000-point level rests on TSMC's global market share, the real revenues of the AI industry, and the long-term capital of 13 million accounts. The difference between a bubble and a bull market is not in the height of the number — it lies in the capital structure.[^11]

But the risks have not disappeared — they have only changed shape. TSMC's extreme concentration at 45% of the index, the threat of geopolitical tensions to semiconductor supply chains, and the volatility that comes when day-trading volume occasionally exceeds 40% of total turnover — all are structural hazards in this 30,000-point building.

> **✦** "In 1990, 4.6 million accounts pushed an island to ten thousand points, then it shattered. In 2026, 13 million accounts stand quietly at 30,000 points. Most hands aren't holding hot tips — they're holding ETFs."

From eighteen companies in 1962 to more than 1,050 in 2026. In sixty-four years, Taiwan's capital market has grown from a small general store into the world's tenth-largest stock exchange. Along the road, people died, people went mad, people grew rich. But perhaps the greatest change lies not in the index, but in people's minds: has that era of "don't touch stocks" truly passed? Just ask your parents.
