# Taiwan's White Terror

> **30-Second Overview:** From 1949 to 1987 — 38 years and 56 days — Taiwan recorded at least 29,407 military tribunal cases, more than 20,000 confirmed victims, with popular estimates reaching 140,000 to 200,000. But what sustained this system for 38 years was not the secret agents — there were only a few thousand. It was the system of "mutual surety": every Taiwanese who wanted to work, attend school, or marry had to find neighbors willing to vouch for them. The old man running the corner grocery store was your surveillance device.

---

On the morning of May 28, 1963, at Taipei's Machangding execution ground, 46-year-old Chen Chih-hsiung was dragged from his cell to the killing field. The Garrison Command guards knew he would shout slogans, so they first hacked off the soles of his feet with an axe so he could not stand, then stuffed his mouth with a rag and drove iron wire through both his cheeks.[^1]

Yet in the moment before the shots rang out, he still managed to cry out in Japanese: "Long live the Taiwanese people! Long live Taiwan's independence!"

Chen Chih-hsiung was a graduate of Tokyo University of Foreign Studies in the Dutch language program and a former Japanese Foreign Ministry official. After the war, he returned to Taiwan and threw himself into the independence movement. In 1961 he organized the "Tong Xin She" (同心社). Two years later, he became the first Taiwanese to be executed for "advocating Taiwan independence" during the 38-year martial law period. Sixty years after his death, a small group still holds a memorial for him each May 28. Most Taiwanese have never heard his name.

This article is about the machine that sent Chen Chih-hsiung to Machangding, that listed Shih Shui-huan among her Post Office colleagues' names, that lured Kao I-sheng down from Mount Ali, that imprisoned Bo Yang for nine years over a single cartoon — **White Terror was not about who carried it out, but about a system that turned every one of the island's two million families into a mutual surveillance network**.

---

## From 29,407 to 140,000 — Why the Numbers Never Add Up

In November 1988, fourteen months after martial law was lifted, Chief Prosecutor Chen Shou-huang reported to the Legislative Yuan: over the 38 years of martial law, military agencies had issued criminal verdicts against non-serving military personnel in a total of **29,407 cases**.[^2]

This was the first official figure. But all researchers knew it was only the tip of the iceberg.

| 29,407 cases                                | 14,946 entries                                      | 1,061 people                          |
| ------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| Ministry of Justice military tribunal cases | Victims in Transitional Justice Commission database | Confirmed death sentences carried out |

The "Taiwan Transitional Justice Database," launched by the Transitional Justice Commission in 2020, contains records of **14,946** individuals subjected to political case verdicts.[^3] The Taiwan Association for Truth and Reconciliation tallied **1,061** death-row inmates executed during martial law (as of 2013).[^4] A 2017 Executive Yuan estimate put the actual number of victims at potentially over **200,000**.[^5]

These numbers will never reconcile. The reason is not different statistical methodologies — it is that they are fundamentally impossible to count: how many people died fleeing, how many were secretly executed, how many were killed resisting arrest — the files contain no record. Tsai Kuan-yu of the Taiwan Association for Truth and Reconciliation noted that victims of mainland Chinese origin accounted for 46% of the total.[^5] This overturns the common impression that White Terror was the KMT targeting native Taiwanese. Once the machine was turned on, anyone could be fed into it.

> **⚠️ Contested Territory**
> The number of White Terror victims remains a political question, not a statistical one. Conservatives tend to cite the Ministry of Justice's 29,407, emphasizing that most cases were documented; independence advocates cite 140,000 to 200,000, emphasizing the unknowable toll of those eliminated underground. Both figures are partially true, because the "truth" of that era was itself a systematically hidden commodity.

## Mutual Surety — What Sustained 38 Years of Martial Law

In the year Chen Chih-hsiung was executed, Taiwan's population was approximately 12 million. How many were secret agents? Based on estimates from historical sources, the actual personnel of intelligence systems including the Garrison Command, Security Command, Investigation Bureau, and the China Youth Corps numbered in the thousands.

So what sustained 38 years?

**Every Taiwanese who wanted to work, attend school, or get married had to find two people willing to vouch for them.**

This system was codified in the 1950 "Statute for the Suppression of Bandits and Spies During the Period of Communist Rebellion" under the name "co-sponsorship and mutual culpability system."[^4] A prisoner seeking release had to find two guarantors to fill out multiple forms: children could not vouch for parents, guarantors had to have a minimum level of assets, and both personal and commercial guarantees were required. The forms were submitted to the local precinct, then the National Police Agency, then the Ministry of National Defense Military Law Bureau, and finally the prison would issue a release certificate. If the person being guaranteed "re-offended," the guarantors bore joint responsibility.

This mechanism was not limited to prison releases. Civil service appointments, school admissions, overseas travel applications, and marriage registration — every routine act required a "certificate of clean record," and a clean record required someone to vouch for you.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> In household registers from 1950s–1980s Taiwan, many entries carry a red stamp beside the name: the guarantor's name, address, and ID number. Open an old household register today and you see the kinship and neighbor relationships of that era recorded through the lens of political liability. Why did your neighbor remember you? Because he vouched for you. Why did your uncle stop speaking to your father for a period? Because he refused to vouch for your father. Those silences, ruptures, and entanglements in family histories — many began with a single guarantee form.

### Eyes Beyond the Agents

In addition to the mutual surety system, there was a network of informants. In 1983, university campuses across Taiwan had more than **5,000 informants**.[^6] From 1980 to 2000, between **7,000 and 15,000 citizens** were under government surveillance each year.[^6]

Reports written by these informants recorded the sexual orientations, extramarital affairs, and secret vices of dissidents. Files discussed using "female psychological vulnerabilities" to undermine radical performers.[^6] Information could be fabricated, exaggerated, deployed as a tool in power struggles — but once it entered the files, it was real.

> **✦** "There is a secret agent watching me at the door. I have to escape." — White Terror survivor Chen Meng-he, speaking to a visitor during a late-life hospital stay (quoted in The Reporter's "Searching for Those Wounded by Politics")

Chen Meng-he was a political prisoner held for over a decade. After his release he became a photographer, documenting the images of other survivors. But sixty years later, lying in a hospital bed, he still believed there was an agent at the door.[^7] You cannot say he was wrong. The greatest achievement of White Terror was making people remember it for the rest of their lives.

## Three Types of Arrest: Thought, Association, Bad Luck

The most terrifying thing about White Terror was not that it had clear criteria — it was that it had none.

### Thought: Bo Yang's One Cartoon (1968)

On January 3, 1968, the family edition of the China Daily News published a translation of the American comic strip "Popeye" by translator Bo Yang.[^8] The story had Popeye and his son jointly purchase an island and establish a private nation, each running for president. Bo Yang's translation had the child say to Popeye: "The whole country is just us two, you know!"

The military tribunal determined this line alluded to the two Chiangs (Chiang Kai-shek and Chiang Ching-kuo). On March 7, Bo Yang was arrested and sentenced to 12 years by military tribunal. The death of Chiang Kai-shek in 1975 reduced his sentence to 8 years, and on the day his sentence expired in March 1976, the National Security Bureau designated him for transfer to Green Island for continued "supervised employment." It was not until April 1977, after the U.S. government expressed concern, that he was released.[^8]

Total: 9 years and 26 days. For translating an American cartoon.

> **💡 Did You Know**
> During his nine years on Green Island, Bo Yang studied the "Zizhi Tongjian" intensively, completing the manuscripts for "An Outline of Chinese History," "A Genealogy of Chinese Emperors, Empresses, Princes and Princesses," and "A Chronology of Chinese History." After release he spent another decade translating the 72-volume "Bo Yang Edition of the Zizhi Tongjian." That prison cell transformed a translator into a historian.

### Association: Shih Shui-huan's Ceiling (1954)

Shih Shui-huan was born in Tainan in 1926, graduated from Tainan Home Economics Girls' School, and worked as a clerk at the Taipei Post and Telecommunications Bureau. In 1954 she was 28 years old.

Her younger brother Shih Chih-cheng was a National Taiwan University student who began fleeing after being implicated in the "NTU Branch Case," and hid in the ceiling of her Taipei dormitory, **hiding there for two years**. On July 19, 1954, Shih Shui-huan was arrested because she had sheltered her brother, and because colleagues Qian Jing-zhi and Ding Yao-tiao were swept into the "Post and Telecommunications Branch Case."[^9] Two years later, on July 24, 1956, she was executed in Taipei at the age of 30. Her brother Shih Chih-cheng's ultimate fate remains unknown.

During her two years in prison, she wrote 69 letters home to her mother.[^10]

> **✦** "Dear Mother, tonight I don't know why my heart is so overwhelmed with grief that it takes my breath away, and tears keep welling up in my eyes. But I am still biting my lips and forcing myself to hold on, because I know I should not cry for Mother's sake, as it would only add to her sorrow..." — Shih Shui-huan, letter from prison (quoted in the Taiwan Association for Truth and Reconciliation's "Shih Shui-huan's Letters")

In her last letter she wrote: "Each morning as Mother instructed, I read the Bible and pray. May God's grace descend upon our whole family. Amen!"[^10]

Her charge was "communicating with the enemy." In reality, her crime was having a brother who was a fugitive.

### Bad Luck: The 896 Villagers of Lugu (1952)

On December 28–29, 1952, the Ministry of National Defense mobilized thousands of troops and police to surround the Lugu mountain area of Shiding Township, Taipei County (present-day Guangming Village, Shiding District, New Taipei City), in a massive sweep for the alleged "Taiwan People's Armed Defense Corps." The underground organization led by commander Chen Ben-jiang and branch secretary Chen Chun-ching did genuinely exist, but most of the villagers they had recruited had no idea what they had joined — some had simply given the fugitives a meal.[^11]

896 people were arrested. 135 were sentenced; 41 received death sentences.[^11] Compensation eventually paid totaled 545.63 million NT dollars; the Control Yuan censured the Ministry of National Defense.[^11] This was the largest single political case of the White Terror period.

Former National History Museum director Chang Yen-hsien spent years walking through Lugu, conducting oral history interviews with over 100 villagers.[^11] A common thread: they could not fully explain "what exactly happened that year." Not because they had forgotten, but because they had never truly known. Some people did not find out until years after their release what organization they had "joined."

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Director Wu Nien-jen's films "A City of Sadness" and "Dust in the Wind" drew partially on the Lugu story. The site today is called the "Lugu Incident Memorial Park" and contains a commemorative stele. In 2017, at a memorial ceremony, the son of principal figure Chen Ben-jiang said: "I apologize on behalf of my father." Perpetrators' descendants offering public apologies — this is extremely rare in White Terror commemoration.

## The Bunks on Green Island and Kao I-sheng's Letters

In 1951, the majority of political prisoners across Taiwan were concentrated and sent to Green Island's "New Life Correction Center" (新生訓導處) for ideological reeducation.[^12] This institution operated until 1965 and held up to **2,000 people** at its peak, organized into 3 battalions and 12 companies, each company holding 120 to 160 people.[^12]

Prisoners (collectively called "New Lives") spent three hours daily on ideological reeducation: the teachings of the Father of the Nation, the leader's words and deeds, the Three Principles of the People, communist atrocities, and critiques of communism. The remaining hours were for labor: building structures, repairing roads, growing vegetables.

Survivor Chang Tse-chou later revisited the reconstructed barracks: "There weren't enough bunks, and many people had to sleep on the floor — I was often woken up by people stepping on me!"[^12]

Between 1953 and 1956, the New Life Correction Center was also the site of a "second rebellion case": inmates already serving sentences on Green Island were accused of continuing to organize in prison, tried again, and given heavier sentences.[^12] Some were kept on Green Island for an additional decade or more as a result.

### Kao I-sheng's 60 Letters

Kao I-sheng (Tsou name: Uyongu Yatauyungana, 1908–1954) was a Tsou indigenous leader, musician, educator, and the first democratically elected township head of Wufeng Township (now Alishan Township). On September 10, 1952, he was lured down from Mount Ali by the pretext of a "Mountain Security Council Meeting" and imprisoned in the Military Law Detention Center at No. 3, Qingdao East Road, Taipei.[^13]

During two years in prison, he wrote 60 letters in Japanese to his family back in Alishan.[^13] On April 17, 1954, he and five others were executed in Taipei. The charge was "communist conspiracy and rebellion."

The letters were mostly about ordinary matters: grow the rice properly, look after the children, trust in God. Half a year before his execution he wrote: "If I can return home safely, I will continue working for my people."

On World Human Rights Day in 2013, Kao I-sheng's son Kao Ying-chieh donated these 60 letters to the National Human Rights Museum. In 2020, the Ministry of Culture officially published "Kao I-sheng's Prison Letters" — the first published book on **indigenous transitional justice** in Taiwan.[^14] Only half a century later could the Tsou people finally read the complete voice of their leader in his final days.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> At the donation ceremony, Kao Ying-chieh said he had read those letters for decades and only now felt he truly understood them — because his father wrote in Japanese, and Kao's generation grew up under a KMT government that forbade speaking Japanese. White Terror did not only kill a Tsou leader; it severed two generations' shared language. A father's words the son could not read — this is the deepest form of violence White Terror inflicted.

## Why This History Has Not Ended

It is easy to say "White Terror is over." Martial law ended in 1987, the Punishments for Sedition Statutes were abolished in 1991, a compensation foundation was established in 1995, and the Transitional Justice Commission launched in 2018. The state machine has not killed anyone for 38 years.

But open the family history of any Taiwanese born between the 1950s and 1980s, and turn to the pages about their parents. The phrase "acting as guarantor" (作保) appears often, followed by a pause. Their uncle refused to vouch for someone; their neighbor once vouched for someone; their grandfather nearly lost his job for lacking a guarantor. That pause is where White Terror has not ended.

Chen Chih-hsiung was shot at Machangding in 1963. Kao I-sheng was shot in Taipei in 1954. Shih Shui-huan was shot in Taipei in 1956. Their families spent half a century before they dared speak these names publicly. Taiwan's transitional justice has overturned 5,983 guilty verdicts,[^3] established memorial parks, erected commemorative steles.

But the descendants of those 5,000 campus informants still live, work, and vote in Taiwan. What their fathers or grandfathers did — no lustration law requires them to account for it.[^6] The so-called "past" has never been publicly reconciled.

This is not about hatred. It is about an open ledger. The day White Terror truly ends will not be the day martial law was lifted, nor the day the Transitional Justice Commission dissolved. It will be when Taiwanese society is willing to acknowledge: the system that turned two million families into a mutual surveillance network — its traces are still etched into the unease we feel toward our neighbors, toward strangers, toward those two words, "acting as guarantor."
