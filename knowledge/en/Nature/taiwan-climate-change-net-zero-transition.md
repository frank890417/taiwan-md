# Taiwan's Climate Crisis and Net-Zero Transition

> **30-second overview:** Taiwan's average temperature has risen 1.6°C over the past century — nearly half again the global average. Academia Sinica climatologist Hsu Huang-hsiung has warned that in the worst-case scenario, Taiwan could lose its winter after 2060. At the same time, this island — which depends on imports for 98% of its energy — is pursuing the largest energy transition in its history. Renewable energy's share of power generation broke 11.6% in 2024, installed offshore wind capacity has reached 4.5 GW, and the government has mapped out NT$9 trillion in investment to achieve net-zero emissions by 2050. But the costs of transition are becoming visible: Changhua fishermen are suing the government for "eliminating fishing communities," the 513 power outage exposed the fragility of the grid, and the carbon credit exchange opened two years ago and is still feeling its way toward price discovery.

In the summer of 2017, Hsu Huang-hsiung of the Academia Sinica's Research Center for Environmental Changes stared at data on his screen and made a prediction his colleagues were reluctant to say publicly: if emission trends continued unchanged, Taiwan's winter might entirely disappear after 2060.[^1] Not shorten — disappear. Winter days would drop to zero, and summer would stretch to seven months.

This is not science fiction. From 1911 to 2020, Taiwan's annual mean temperature has already risen 1.6°C — nearly one and a half times the global average over the same period (1.1°C).[^1] Days above 35°C in Taipei have surged from about three per year in the 1960s to fifteen per year in recent decades.[^2] The situation is worse in the south: Tainan and Kaohsiung already see more than thirty extreme heat days per year.

In the same building, Wang Zhong-ho at the Institute of Earth Sciences was calculating another set of numbers. His conclusions were equally unsettling: the rate of sea level rise around Taiwan is twice the global average.[^3] Multiple simulations indicate that sea level rise combined with storm surge will expose Taiwan's western coastal lowlands to greater flooding risk; among the six special municipalities, areas like New Taipei, Tainan, and Kaohsiung have particularly high numbers of people and land area exposed to these risks.

Hsu Huang-hsiung and Wang Zhong-ho's research points to the same conclusion: Taiwan is experiencing climate change faster than almost anywhere else on Earth.

## When the Rain Stops Following the Rules

Temperature is only a surface symptom. The deeper change is hidden in rainfall.

Taiwan's total rainfall hasn't clearly decreased, but the rain's behavior has changed: it doesn't fall when it should, and when it does, it pours. Spring rainfall has dropped sharply; dry seasons are drier. In 2021, Taiwan experienced its worst drought in fifty-six years, with reservoir levels hitting historic lows, and TSMC at one point dispatching water trucks to its factories.[^4] That same year, two major power outages struck the island in succession in May.

The frequency of extreme rainfall events is also climbing. Days with rainfall exceeding 200 millimeters have increased from a yearly average of five in the 1960s to eight in recent years. In 2009, Typhoon Morakot set a cumulative rainfall record of 2,884 millimeters at Alishan[^5] — the rain that fell in three days equaled Taipei's entire annual rainfall. In that disaster, the village of Xiaolin in Kaohsiung's Jiaxian District was buried under a landslide from Xiandu Mountain in the early morning hours, killing 491 people.[^6]

> "Each chair represents a family member." Survivor Wang Min-liang later said this to visitors at the Xiaolin Memorial Park. He founded the Sunlight Xiaolin community and leads the Taivoan people's cultural song and dance troupe on tours across Taiwan. (From Public Television Service's "Our Island")

Morakot was not an accident — it was a preview. The 2024 National Climate Change Scientific Report, chaired by Hsu Huang-hsiung, states: extreme rainfall events that currently occur once every fifty years may in the future occur once every ten years.[^7] Yunlin, Tainan, and Keelung are the areas at highest risk for coastal flooding.

## The Bill for the World's 22nd Largest Emitter

Taiwan is not just a victim of climate change — it is also a contributor.

For an island of twenty-three million people, Taiwan's carbon emissions are disproportionately large: measured by fossil fuel CO₂ emissions, annual output is approximately 280 million tonnes, with a per capita figure of roughly 11.7 tonnes — placing Taiwan in the world's top tier. Depending on the database and accounting methodology used, Taiwan ranks somewhere around twenty-something globally.[^8] Emissions are heavily concentrated in energy use and electricity supply, with the energy sector making up the largest share; the power generation mix remains central to the challenge of emissions reduction.

The root of the problem lies in the generation mix. In 2024, Taiwan's power generation structure was approximately: natural gas 42.4%, coal 39.3% (gas for the first time exceeded coal), renewables approximately 11.6%, and nuclear approximately 4.2%.[^20] This is an energy system still heavily dependent on fossil fuels — and one in which 98% of energy is imported. Energy security and the climate crisis are the same problem.

The industrial structure makes matters more complicated. Taiwan's manufacturing sector is dominated by energy-intensive industries; just four sectors — steel, petrochemicals, cement, and paper — account for sixty percent of industrial emissions. These are predominantly export-oriented industries, and the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), entering its formal implementation phase in 2026, will raise the cost of carbon for high-carbon products such as steel, cement, aluminum, fertilizers, electricity, and hydrogen exported to Europe, along with increased disclosure requirements.[^9]

On Earth Day, April 22, 2021, President Tsai Ing-wen spoke the sentence that has been quoted ever since: "Net-zero transition by 2050 is the world's goal, and Taiwan's goal."[^10] This was more than a political statement — the following year, the National Development Council released a comprehensive roadmap laying out twelve key strategies and estimating NT$9 trillion in required investment.

Taiwan had formally committed to a transition it cannot afford to fail.

## The Wind in the Strait

The Taiwan Strait is one of the best wind resource sites in the world. This geographic fact has made offshore wind power the central bet of the energy transition.

From near zero in 2016, Taiwan's cumulative installed offshore wind capacity reached approximately 4.5 GW by March 2026,[^11] with expansion speed among the fastest in Asia. Denmark's Ørsted completed the Greater Changhua Southwest Phase 2 and Northwest wind farms off the coast of Changhua, with a combined capacity of 920 MW.[^12] The third phase of block development, launched in 2026, has an allocated capacity for this round of 3.6 GW, with a target of completion and grid connection between 2030 and 2031.[^11]

The government's blueprint is even larger: 13 GW of offshore wind by 2030, with an ambition of 55 GW by 2050.

But the turbines at sea bring not only electricity — they also bring conflict. In February 2022, more than a hundred Changhua fishermen marched to the Executive Yuan to protest, accusing the government of "eliminating fishing communities" for wind power.[^13] The restricted navigation zones designated for offshore wind farms blocked off sea areas where they had worked for generations. This was not an emotional protest — in May 2025, a court ruled that the navigation zone restrictions were unlawful, marking the first time in Taiwan that a court had challenged the spatial governance of offshore wind.[^14]

> The fishermen were not saying "no wind power." They were saying "why should we bear the cost of transition?" That question should have been answered before the turbine foundations were poured — but it wasn't taken seriously until the gavel fell.

Solar power followed a different path. Solar installed capacity reached 14,281 MW in 2024, accounting for sixty-eight percent of total renewables and generating 14.9 billion kWh.[^15] Rooftop, ground-mounted, floating, and agrivoltaic systems — diverse installation types made solar the workhorse of renewables.

But solar has its own battleground. The agrivoltaic policy has drawn criticism for "fake farming, real electricity." Some farmers have set aside agricultural production to focus on solar income, leading the Ministry of Agriculture to step up inspections. On an island where cropland totals only 790,000 hectares, every plot's use is a political question.

## That Afternoon on 5/13

At 2:37 p.m. on May 13, 2021, at the Lubei ultra-high-voltage substation serving Hsing-Ta Power Plant in Kaohsiung, an operator opened switch 3541 — the one he should have opened was 3542.[^16]

This human error triggered a busbar ground fault; four generating units tripped; and power capacity instantaneously dropped by 2.2 GW. From 3 p.m. onward, rolling power cuts were implemented across Taiwan in six rounds of fifty minutes each, affecting approximately four million households. Making matters worse, solar generation dwindled as the sun set, and the drought had already reduced hydroelectric output. The situation was not resolved until coal-fired units came back online around 7 p.m. and power was fully restored by 8 p.m.

Four days later on May 17, Hsing-Ta Unit 1 failed again, bringing a second wave of cuts. Between the two events, more than 5.62 million households were affected.[^16]

The May 13 and May 17 events exposed not just human error but how fragile a power system in mid-transition can be. Renewables are inherently intermittent: when the sun goes down, there's no power; when the wind stops, there's no power. The government's solution is storage: plans call for 1.5 GW of battery storage by 2025, expanding to 8.6 GW by 2030. But storage costs remain high and the technology is still maturing.

This is the most honest face of the energy transition: the old system is already insufficient; the new system isn't ready yet.

## Putting a Price on Carbon

On August 7, 2023, Taiwan's Carbon Credit Exchange was officially established in the Asia New Bay Area of Kaohsiung, with an initial paid-in capital of NT$1 billion and a planned capital of NT$1.5 billion; the Taiwan Stock Exchange contributed NT$600 million and the National Development Fund NT$400 million.[^17] On December 22 of that year, the first batch of international carbon credit transactions was completed — forty-five companies purchased approximately 88,500 tonnes of CO₂ equivalent in international carbon credits for more than USD$800,000.[^17]

In 2025, Taiwan's domestic carbon fee system went into formal effect, marking Taiwan's entry into its "inaugural year of carbon pricing."[^18] Formosa Plastics' energy efficiency improvement project was listed at NT$3,000 per tonne; Hanbao Animal Husbandry's biogas power generation project was priced at NT$3,000–4,000. But the market is still finding its footing: trading volumes have been low, and most companies consider domestic carbon credit prices too high.

Meanwhile, tech giants like TSMC and Foxconn have already been fighting in another arena. Under the RE100 initiative, these companies have committed to using 100% renewable energy. TSMC plans to achieve net-zero emissions by 2050. When international clients treat green power as a supply chain requirement, green power supply is no longer an environmental question — it is an industrial survival question.

In his 2024 National Day address, President Lai Ching-te announced the launch of a "Second Energy Transition," covering three directions: diversified green energy, deep energy efficiency, and advanced energy storage.[^19] The targets he set: renewables reaching 20% of generation by 2026, and 30% by 2030. But by 2025, renewables' share remained clearly below the original 20% target; depending on the accounting methodology, it was approximately 12.7% to 13.1%.[^20] The Ministry of Economic Affairs has since revised its estimate, saying the 20% mark is expected to be achievable from November 2026, with approximately 30% by 2030.

## More Than Cutting Emissions

Reducing emissions is only half the job. The other half is adapting to changes that have already happened.

Taiwan's cities are learning to become "sponges." Taipei, New Taipei, Taichung, and Kaohsiung have formulated climate adaptation plans, increasing rainwater retention and infiltration capacity and alleviating the urban heat island effect. Chiayi County's government has already launched relocation assistance programs for high-risk coastal areas — an acknowledgment that some places will ultimately not be suitable for habitation.

Agriculture is also adapting. The Taiwan Agricultural Research Institute is cultivating drought- and heat-resistant crop varieties and promoting precision irrigation. But climate change's impact on agriculture and fisheries is comprehensive: mango and lychee yields have become unstable due to insufficient low-temperature periods; the composition of nearshore fish species is changing and traditional fishing grounds are shifting northward; coral bleaching devastated Kenting, Green Island, and Orchid Island in 2020.[^21]

Taiwan is not a UN member and cannot sign the Paris Agreement as a nation. But Taiwanese companies and cities participate in international climate action through non-governmental frameworks such as RE100 and the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi), claiming a place in the global emissions reduction map through another channel.

## The NT$9 Trillion Bet

In May 2025, that Changhua court ruling sent shockwaves through the energy sector. The court said fishermen's rights cannot be sacrificed for wind power — but offshore wind remains an indispensable piece of the net-zero puzzle. The law resolved one case without resolving the underlying question: who bears the cost of transition?

Taiwan's net-zero transition is an endeavor fighting both physical laws and political reality simultaneously. Physically, this island is warming faster than the globe, its typhoons are stronger than before, and its sea level is rising faster than elsewhere. Politically, every wind turbine, every solar panel, every incremental rise in the electricity bill carries the weight of votes.

In Hsu Huang-hsiung's models, Taiwan after 2060 has no winter. In coastal risk assessments, western lowland areas face increasing exposure to sea level rise and storm surge. And offshore Changhua, turbine blades are still turning, fishing boats are still heading out.

Whether NT$9 trillion can buy a different future, no one knows. But the consequences of not spending that money — those we are already beginning to see.
