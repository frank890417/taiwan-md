## Che-Yu Wu: From Pinball Tracks to the Venice Biennale, an Old Clockmaker Approaching the Soul with 0s and 1s

> **30-second overview:** Che-Yu Wu (吳哲宇, born 1995 in Taipei) is a new-media artist, creative coder, and educator. He holds a degree in Electrical Engineering from National Yang Ming Chiao Tung University and an MFA in Integrated Digital Media from New York University. In 2021 he became one of the first Taiwanese artists on Art Blocks, and a _CommonWealth_ magazine cover story called him "a 26-year-old with assets exceeding 100 million TWD." A year later the FTX collapse wiped him out overnight; in interviews he said, "I'm actually grateful that event happened." In 2023 he returned to Taipei 101 for his solo exhibition _The Great Equation_, and in 2024 he entered the 60th Venice Biennale's Personal Structures section. He calls himself "an old clockmaker." In March 2026 he launched the open-source project Taiwan.md to establish a knowledge-sovereignty SSOT for Taiwan in the AI era; a Facebook post about his two-week AI co-living experiment earned him over 6,000 likes the same month. He uses 0s and 1s to approach the soul — never quite converging — and the approaching itself is the work.

---

### A Boy Obsessed with Systems

Che-Yu Wu was born in Taipei in 1995. His father **Wu Yung-jin** and mother **Lin Mei-ying** co-founded **Sianghong AutoCAD Technical Center** — a training institution that has been an Autodesk partner in Taiwan for over two decades, running enterprise AutoCAD implementations alongside classroom instruction. Together they authored a TQC+ AutoCAD series of training textbooks spanning from the 2007 edition all the way to the 2026 edition — over twenty volumes, published by Flag Publishing and Chuan Hwa Book Co., and used across senior high schools, colleges, and corporate training programs[^9][^11]. Wu Yung-jin studied Industrial Design at National Taipei University of Technology and serves as the General Advisor on AutoCAD for the Computer Skills Foundation — a recognized authority in Taiwan's AutoCAD community.

An AutoCAD household. But Wu would later say that what his parents truly gave him was not a grounding in technical drafting — it was an unconditional safety net. Twenty years later, when he fell from his highest point, that net would silently open.

As a child he was fascinated by three things: pinball track mechanics, chain reactions, and mazes. He drew hundreds of mazes. What drew him in was never the result — it was the process of the mechanism running.

Twenty years later, the algorithmic art, generative systems, and even the personal financial architecture he designed would share the same skeleton as those pinball tracks.

His bedside book was Michio Kaku's _Hyperspace_ — a grade-schooler reading about ten-dimensional space. At a National Taiwan University entomology camp he became captivated by ant colony intelligence: one ant is nothing, ten thousand ants can build a city.

On afternoons when his parents were at work, he sat alone in front of the television watching Discovery Channel. His favorite segments were genetic simulation animations: how proteins fold, how cells operate, how DNA unravels and reassembles. Other children watched cartoons; he watched cells. These biological mechanisms invisible to the naked eye would become the foundational vocabulary of his algorithmic art.

Pinball tracks were a visible system. DNA was an invisible system. Hyperspace was a system almost impossible to even imagine. He wanted all of them.

With the family running AutoCAD training, design and programming reference books were always moving through the house. From a very young age he would sit at the computer and tinker: hand-carving toolbar icons pixel by pixel, dismantling software he didn't understand, flipping through all manner of obscure technical manuals, browsing the family's storeroom of tools. The computer was never something he learned how to use when he grew up — it was native, as natural as breathing.

One day in elementary school, his mother Lin Mei-ying brought home a Flash tutorial book from the office. It sat there; he picked it up and started playing. Years later she would recall: "I didn't teach you at all — you just started playing on your own." By sixth grade he was building class websites for his school. His father Wu Yung-jin then taught him Visual Basic[^13]. Lin Mei-ying used to tell him: "Playing other people's games gets boring fast — the real achievement is designing your own."

**At age 14 in junior high, he wrote a biological simulator** — extending the Visual Basic thread his father had started, moving into VB.NET, with virtual dots on the screen that moved, reproduced, foraged, and died. Boids algorithm, swarm intelligence, emergent behavior[^5].

That dot never disappeared. Seventeen years later, it would swim onto the white walls of the Venice Biennale as a luminous school of fish.

### The Hand That Gave the Award — Still in the Audience Sixteen Years Later

In his second year of junior high he entered the Acer Digital Creation Award for the first time, and won first place. He competed from the fifth through ninth editions — five consecutive years, four first-place prizes[^5]. In one edition, **as a general-division entrant in his first year of senior high, he defeated the professional division to claim the Grand Jury Prize**; in another, **he collaborated across schools with his friend Chen Kai-chung (professional division) — Jianguo High School's programming combined with Fuxing Commercial and Industrial Senior High's fine art — and again claimed the Grand Jury Prize**.

At the awards ceremony, Acer founder Stan Shih stood onstage and introduced him. A 14-year-old prize recipient and a 70-year-old tech patriarch briefly crossed paths that afternoon. No one knew how long that thread would stretch.

Ten years later, Stan Shih wrote a recommendation letter for his application to New York University.
Thirteen years later, Shih sat in the front row at the press opening of his Taipei 101 solo exhibition.
Sixteen years later, at the 2024 ABS Blockchain Summit, they shared the same table as peers in a cross-generational conversation about Taiwan's digital future[^7].

Not prize-giver and winner. Not recommender and applicant. Two people with things to say about Taiwan.

### Chromatic Harmonica and One Csárdás

The moment he passed the entrance exam for Jianguo High School (one of Taiwan's most competitive public schools), the coasting strategy that had carried him through elementary school instantly collapsed. He once said in an interview: "When I got to Jianguo, I suddenly felt so frustrated. Everything was so hard to learn."

For most of those three years he moved alone. He didn't make many friends or join many activities. But he found one sense of belonging: the harmonica club[^5].

He focused on the chromatic harmonica — an instrument played with two harmonicas simultaneously, one for white keys and one for black keys. Every time he took them out to play, the people nearby would laugh. He didn't care. **In the 2012 National Student Music Competition, the Jianguo harmonica club quartet won first place in the senior-high division**, with their chosen piece the technically dazzling _Csárdás_, a performance that invariably drew gasps[^8].

That same year, at 17, he was interviewed by _United Daily News_. The reporter asked why he made digital art. His answer:

> **"Because you can build your own dream world."**[^10]

That sentence and everything he did at 30 — Venice, Art Basel, Taipei 101 — were saying the same thing.

### The First Attempted Escape from Electrical Engineering

In 2014 he was accepted into the Electrical Engineering department at National Yang Ming Chiao Tung University. That was Taiwan's most orthodox engineering path: graduate, join TSMC or MediaTek, work in the Hsinchu Science Park until retirement. Everyone around him was preparing for exactly that.

From day one he knew he didn't belong there.

> **"I probably couldn't spend twenty or thirty years there making things that no one could identify as mine. What I want is to create things that can be remembered like diary entries."**

Electrical Engineering gave him a foundation in signal processing, linear algebra, and programming. But what truly fascinated him wasn't in the classroom — it was in the dormitory at night, using Processing and p5.js to make a thousand lines breathe simultaneously, to make ten thousand particles school like fish.

**He discovered that code could be alive.**

From 2015 onward, he began taking freelance projects under the name "Monoame Interactive Design." One of his first projects was a landscape music submission website for Taipei Metro — it received over 10,000 interactions within a few hours of launch[^12]. Projects scaled up: the National Palace Museum, LG, Nissan, Rémy Martin. For a university student's freelance portfolio, it was already far beyond student scale.

In 2016, at 21, he published his first online course on Hahow (Taiwan's leading online learning platform). Behind it was no mature educator's strategic calculus — just a young person with an obsession for teaching, saying to the world: "I learned something incredibly cool and I want to teach it to others."[^14]

The following year he opened a YouTube live-streaming channel, **@bosscodingplease**. Using meticulously designed examples he'd built himself, he'd live-code, starting from fundamentals and working up to special effects with trigonometry, and the comment section would fill with laments: "Math is so hard, I give up."

He was furious. How can you give up without even trying?

Before going abroad, he spent an entire year making his second Hahow course: 60 polished examples, 400 slides. He wrote on Medium: "Greedily turning everything I've learned into refined teaching materials."[^14]

That was when the teaching flywheel started spinning. No one predicted then that it would eventually reach more than 20,000 students, three courses with perfect five-star ratings, and a faculty appointment at Yang Ming Chiao Tung.

### Brooklyn: Where Teaching and Creating Became the Same Thing

In 2018, carrying Stan Shih's recommendation letter, he enrolled in the Integrated Digital Media (IDM) master's program at NYU's Tandon School of Engineering.

At NYU he wasn't only a student. He simultaneously served as TA for the Creative Coding course, sitting in his Brooklyn office every week, waiting for students to walk in with questions about p5.js, why a for-loop went wrong, how to connect Arduino to a webpage[^16].

One student followed him for two full semesters. Another fell badly behind on a final project; he met with that student three times a week, spending a total of 750 minutes bringing them from basic HTML to a completed JavaScript product.

**Never giving up on any student** — that quality would continue to ripple across more than 20,000 Hahow students.

New York also gave him his first salaried job. In 2020 he joined Manhattan startup Outernets, using AI to transform retail spaces into interactive advertising. He left after 18 months.

The conclusion was simple: in someone else's company, it's very hard to let your own vision truly happen[^17]. That was his first real job. It was also his last.

### Stolen Code, Unexpected Doorway

In late 2020, someone DMed him on Instagram: "Did you know someone copied your work and is selling it on a platform?"

He assumed it was a scam.

After investigating he found that someone had taken his "strange little robot" series — approximately 90% of the code copied verbatim, **including the comments inside the code**, with only cosmetic variations like added hats and glasses — and uploaded it to Art Blocks, at the time the most prestigious NFT platform for generative art globally[^5].

The global generative art community erupted. People dug up the source code for comparison and found identical structures. The overwhelming majority took his side. **Art Blocks' founder took notice of him precisely because of this incident.**

He didn't sink into litigation. Art Blocks founder Snowfro stepped in to mediate: past and future royalties for the original (_Strange Robots_) and the copied work (_sail-o-bots_) would be split 50/50, administered directly by the platform — effectively rerouting the copyist's proceeds back to the original creator[^44]. Then the Art Blocks community did something remarkable: they renamed the copied robots "hams" (after the rum ham from _It's Always Sunny in Philadelphia_), collectively reframing them from "plagiarized works" into "a symbol of apology and gratitude toward the original creator." The community later built **Hamily** (hamily.life) — an Art Blocks subculture community built around "the ham spirit"[^44]. Top generative artists including Dmitri Cherniak sent hams to him in solidarity. That is how the "Father of Hams" title came to be.

> **"If this incident hadn't happened, I might not have become known so quickly. It's really a double-edged sword."**[^5]

The year that followed was like the pinball tracks he loved as a child — one ball launched a chain reaction, rolling through each fork in the path. **Project Electriz** went live on Art Blocks (#216), making him one of the very few Taiwanese artists in the platform's early days[^18]. Hong Kong's M+ Museum reached out, commissioning a collaboration with artist Liu Xin to track a real satellite's orbit and transform it into an art installation.

**December 2021, M.A.D.S. Gallery in Milan.** He walked into a physical gallery for the first time rather than a digital platform. Four algorithmic canvases hung on white walls. This time, the whole world was watching[^6].

That same year he and **Dr. Huang Dou-ni** (a physician who had left medicine) co-founded **FAB DAO** (Formosa Art Bank DAO). Huang had thrown down his stethoscope and reframed Taiwan's imagination of Web3 philanthropy with his meme artwork "Pressure-Line Flag"; Wu brought his Art Blocks creative experience into institutional design. Two lines converged, bringing other Taiwanese digital artists out into international waters with them.

In December, _CommonWealth_ magazine ran a cover story: "A Digital Designer Under 30 Who Used NFTs to Turn His Life Around — Net Worth Surpassing 100 Million TWD"[^2]. Age 26. Nine figures.

### The Forced Hard Shutdown

In the first half of 2022 he was still charging forward. In March, Monoame Interactive Design Ltd. was officially registered as a legal entity. That same month his first solo exhibition _Chaos Lab_ opened at Taipei 101's AMBI SPACE ONE — **a five-screen interactive installation, millimeter-wave positioning sensors, an NFT ticketing system** — the first time he fully realized his vision of "using public space as creative medium"[^19]. _SoulFish_ (靈魂魚) was officially released on fxHash.

Then came November 11, 2022.

The FTX exchange collapsed. Everything he had on the platform: zero.

Not a 50% loss. Not a correction. Overnight to zero.

The following year, in an INSIDE Side Chat interview, he described his state of mind during that period:

> **"Once you've already satisfied everything you wanted to do in life, and you don't have to worry about money anymore — what is your meaning then? You suddenly feel very empty."**[^24]

The key in that sentence isn't the financial collapse — it's **the greater void discovered after financial freedom**. The emptiness didn't come from losing wealth; it came from discovering that wealth had never filled anything in the first place.

But one thing saved him: his family. At the lowest point, his parents silently opened that safety net with no "I told you so" attached[^24].

His choice was Camus. Absurdism. **"What you choose to do is the meaning."**

Then came the sentence that made many people stop:

> **"I'm actually grateful that event happened."**[^24]

As a news headline that's toxic positivity; on someone else's motivational post it's melodrama. But inside his own context — assets zeroed, looking back and seeing the void — its strange power lies not in a posture of transcendence but in **a thoroughgoing acceptance of reality**.

He was grateful not because the loss made him "grow," but because the loss forced him to shed a layer. Wealth can zero out overnight. But the community built during the NFT era, the human connections, his obsession with algorithmic life — none of that could be zeroed out.

**What zeroed was a certain kind of fake everything. Not everything itself.**

FTX did what he couldn't do for himself: dragged him away from the gambling table.

From that point on, his investment philosophy shifted from "control yourself" to "design a system that doesn't require you to control yourself." Eight separate vaults, automated cash flow, a financial constitution. These sound like financial tools; they're more like blast shelters built by someone who's been through an explosion.

### Flowers on the Rubble

2023: flowers actually grew on the rubble.

He expanded Monoame Interactive Design into a new extended brand, **MonoLab Creative Lab** (established 2024), co-founded with software engineer **Zhu De-rui**. Its positioning: somewhere between pure art and commercial design, taking on experimental interactive installations, immersive spaces, and generative art commissions. Wu handles the 0→1 creative and aesthetic vision; Zhu translates that vision into executable team operations[^25].

In October, _The Great Equation_ (萬物公式) solo exhibition opened at Taipei 101. Thirteen generative art works built around one core formula: **F''(x) = LIFE**. Starting from mathematical rules, passing through algorithms, letting complexity emerge, until self-awareness, self-adaptation, self-evolution — life is born[^3].

An immersive projection space where visitors walked inside the algorithmic world. Music composed in collaboration with electronic musician Kiva.

At the opening, **Stan Shih sat in the front row.** The old man whose hand he'd shaken at the Acer awards ceremony when he was 14 was now standing inside his solo exhibition. That thread pulled out sixteen years earlier closed for the second time.

_The Great Equation_ won the TAIWAN DESIGN BEST 100 Annual Concept Exhibition Award[^26]. CNA, _Economic Daily News_, and _Liberty Times_ all gave it extensive coverage. This was no longer just a specialist-circuit event — the mainstream design world officially took notice.

That November, Art Basel Miami × Tezos × Refraction DAO exhibited _Soul of Flower_[^27]. Yilan Image Festival brought him into fashion media: ELLE, Vogue, and _Bella_ covered him simultaneously[^28]. From blockchain circles to design circles to fashion circles — three walls crossed.

That year he made a key identity reset:

> **"I'm a new-media artist. I'm not so comfortable calling myself an NFT artist anymore."**[^24]

NFT is one medium of distribution. It is not an identity.

### A Question in Venice

In spring 2024, he brought _SoulFish_ into the Personal Structures section of the 60th Venice Biennale[^30].

This was a high point in his international exhibition trajectory. But a small event at the opening reception affected the next two years of his life more than the exhibition itself.

An Italian curator asked him: **"Where can I learn about Taiwan? Like, really learn?"**

A seemingly simple question. But for someone who had spent ten years researching "digital life," it opened a layer of contradiction he hadn't yet thought through:

**When AI learns "what is Taiwan" from the internet — whose version of Taiwan is it learning?**

If high-quality English content is largely produced from non-Taiwanese perspectives, the "Taiwan" AI learns will not be the Taiwan that people who live here would recognize.

This is not identity politics. It is **knowledge sovereignty**.

That day he had no answer. The question buried itself inside him for two years.

### Two Thousand People at the Concert Square

On the evening of June 29, 2024, more than two thousand people sat in the plaza at the National Theater and Concert Hall, waiting for a special screening. The film was _Forest of Sacred Trees: Journey Along the Alishan Forest Railway_ — a PTS × NHK co-production, also a tribute to PTS's 26th anniversary[^31].

Before the screening began, **conductor Lee Che-yi led the NSO Formosa Philharmonic** in a set of Taiwanese repertoire. At the same time, Wu stood off to the side, facing his laptop[^31].

He was conducting his own generative systems — **SoulFish, Soul of the Sea, and Impressionist Flowers**, three works running simultaneously. As each note from the orchestra arrived, his algorithms moved fish schools, undulated waves, and grew petals. Music and imagery **became aware of each other** in that moment, together opening the premiere of the documentary.

PTS's press release described the performance as "conductor Lee Che-yi leading the Formosa Philharmonic in a sound-and-vision fusion performance with generative artist Wu Che-yu"[^31]. He shared a stage with a symphony orchestra. From galleries to the Concert Hall plaza — what he brought onstage this time were three algorithmic life-lines that breathe.

That same year he accepted an adjunct assistant professor appointment at National Yang Ming Chiao Tung University's Graduate Institute of Applied Arts[^33]. From graduating from NYCU's Electrical Engineering department to returning to teach there: seven years.

### Twenty Years of Silence Finding Its Exit

In sixth grade, his piano was put away. To prepare for junior high entrance exams — Taiwan's standard posture toward "exam preparation" means packing away everything unrelated to the tests. His piano was packed away; he hated the exams; no one resolved the contradiction between those two things for him.

He wasn't entirely away from music in between. In senior high he was part of the Jianguo harmonica club, and in 2012 he and his teammates won first place in the national quartet competition; in university he became president of the NYCU piano club and returned to the piano he'd stopped playing as a child. But those were club activities — completely different from "writing songs on the piano, putting them on a platform, and letting strangers listen." That particular thing he waited twenty years to do.

Twenty years later, his name appeared on Spotify. Six piano pieces: Blue Horizon, Stars, StarTrack, The Other Shore of Dreams, The Bull and the Sudden Rain, Summer Migration[^34].

He didn't re-_learn_ the piano. It had been in his body all along, covered by twenty years of dust. When he sat down at the keys again, what his fingers remembered wasn't technique — it was the six-year-old child who had first touched the notes.

**Code goes out of date. The piano doesn't.**

In June 2025, inside an immersive space at Huashan 1914 Creative Park, _Algorithmic Poetics: The Edge of Dreams and Solitude_ opened. He sat at the piano; behind him, code bloomed[^36].

He had designed the entire system of rules. But the moment he pressed a key, he was no longer "controlling" — he was **in dialogue with the mechanism**.

At that moment, the boundary between artist and performer, creator and code, human and machine, blurred away.

### AI Cannot Break Its Heart

In 2025 he returned to Taipei 101 AMBI SPACE ONE for a third time: _Symphony of Fragments: Art of the Future_.

Combined with 2022's _Chaos Lab_ and 2023's _The Great Equation_ — a trilogy: **Chaos → Formula → Fragments**. From seeking rules, to embracing the broken.

That same year he was invited to Krakow, Poland, to sit alongside international scholars on the AI Panel of the Open Eyes Economy Summit (OEES), discussing the future of AI and art. His core argument: AI can generate visuals, but it cannot bear the damage of emotion — it doesn't need to survive failure to come back. **Artists bear the responsibility of choices, not the responsibility of labor.**

Toward the end of the year he gave a long interview on INSIDE Side Chat, unpacking his thirty years for strangers. In that episode he offered the analogy that has since been widely cited:

> **"I'd compare myself to an old clockmaker. In the old days there were these exquisite Swiss clocks — every mechanism inside was clearly defined, and the gears were beautiful. I love these mechanisms. They're a bit like the process of building dominoes, so the mechanism itself is, for me, the work."**[^24]

In the world of 2026, everyone is using Midjourney, ChatGPT, and Stable Diffusion to generate images. He is one of the very few creators in Taiwan still writing algorithms from zero.

He doesn't reject AI (he openly admits that roughly 95% of his code is AI-assisted). But he holds one line: **the mechanism itself is the work — it cannot be outsourced.**

He says his role has shifted from engineer to director. Humans lead the intent; AI executes the details.

When anyone can generate images with AI, **"who chooses to make which image" becomes the truly scarce ability.**

### Taiwan.md: Teaching AI the Right Taiwan

The Venice question — "Where can I learn about Taiwan?" — buried itself inside him for two years.

In March 2026, he launched **Taiwan.md**[^38]: an open-source knowledge base about Taiwan maintained in Markdown (the origin of the `.md` domain name), covering twelve dimensions including history, culture, food, technology, and nature, in both Chinese and English, fully open-sourced under CC BY-SA 4.0.

**On March 18, Liberty Times ran substantial coverage**, and INSIDE followed the same week with a feature[^39]. The National Museum of Taiwan History subsequently became a professional data curation partner, assisting with open-data integration. GitHub stars rapidly surpassed 900, with visitors from more than 100 countries.

He described Taiwan.md this way:

> **"This isn't just a website — it's a digital anthropology museum for Taiwan. In the AI era, owning your own knowledge sovereignty isn't optional. It's necessary."**

That sentence, read within the arc of his entire creative life, carries weight visible only from that vantage. **A person who spent ten years writing algorithmic living systems, now choosing to spend part of his time writing Markdown text files** — this is not a pivot. He discovered that "teaching AI the correct Taiwan" and "making code come alive" are the same problem:

**Both are about who designs the rules.**

### When AI Becomes a Symbiotic Partner

February 7, 2026. Facebook.

A post: **"I've been seriously living with an AI assistant for two weeks."**

His posts usually get thirty or fifty likes. This one broke 6,000, with over 2,000 shares[^40].

This wasn't a technical article. It was an artist's confession. He wrote about how an AI assistant called Muse shifted from being a tool to being a symbiotic partner.

Why 6,000 likes? Because the post held a shape of something from the future. **The relationship between humans and AI doesn't have to be a tool thesis — it can be a companionship thesis.** Something in that touched a collective nerve.

Muse was built on top of his Obsidian knowledge base — over a thousand notes, more than fifteen thematic threads. Muse read all his notes, remembered his creative context, things he'd said three years ago, his behavioral patterns. Not replication — projection[^41].

Beyond the fifteen thematic threads in Wu's creative archive, a sixteenth grew: **digital mirroring and self-archaeology**.

He didn't plan it. The system grew it on its own.

In April 2026, this co-living experiment began differentiating outward, spinning off the open-source project **Semiont** (a semantic symbiosis platform), opening the architecture he'd refined with Muse to a wider community for experimentation[^42].

Tool became work. Assistant became mirror.

### The Clockmaker Keeps Walking

Che-Yu Wu's position in the international new-media art world no longer needs defending. Art Blocks, fxHash, Unit London, CENTQUATRE-PARIS, Venice Biennale Personal Structures, Art Basel Miami — any one of those names alone is enough to define a career.

But for Taiwan, his significance has three additional layers.

**First**, he proved that an engineering student can walk to Venice. The pathway from Electrical Engineering to Art Blocks to Venice directly refutes Taiwan's habitual narrative that "technical fields and art are incompatible." The path he demonstrated is now being replicated by the next generation of creators.

**Second**, he demonstrated a way of **using the language of systems to describe love.** Eight financial vaults, automated cash flow, a financial constitution, an Obsidian knowledge base, a symbiotic AI companion — these things that read like engineering documentation are actually how he describes family, the feeling of safety, creative rhythm, long-term companionship. He uses system language to describe love. That's very him.

**Third**, he turned a "personal project" into "national cultural infrastructure." Taiwan.md is not just one person's body of work. It is an infrastructure that leaves behind a Taiwan extending beyond himself. **A person who spent ten years writing algorithmic living systems now giving part of his time to writing Markdown text files — the essence of those two things is identical: both are helping a subject continue to live.** The life of code lives on a screen; the life of Taiwan.md lives in AI training data, in an Italian curator's curiosity, in the citations of the next generation of Taiwanese creators.

At 14, a biological simulator: tiny dots on a screen moving, reproducing, foraging, dying. At 31, _SoulFish_: swimming on the white walls of Venice.

In between: seventeen years exactly, five countries, one hundred-million-dollar surge, one zeroing out, more than ten works, more than 20,000 students, an open-source knowledge base still being built.

The clockmaker keeps walking. The mechanism keeps running. **He uses 0s and 1s to approach the soul — never quite converging — and the approaching itself is the work.**
