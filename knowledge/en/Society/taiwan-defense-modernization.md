> **30-second overview:** The 2022 Russia-Ukraine War transformed Taiwan's defense logic. In 2024, mandatory service was restored from four months to one year. In 2025, the Han Kuang 41 exercise ran for a record ten days and nine nights and mobilized 20,000 reservists; that November, the Lai Ching-te government proposed a special budget of NT$1.25 trillion to purchase 200,000 drones. But most of the money on the same list still goes toward tanks, jets, and submarines — expensive conventional equipment. Former Chief of General Staff Lee Hsi-min smiled wryly: "If a tank can be an asymmetric weapon, then what isn't an asymmetric weapon?" Taiwan is walking two defense roads at once, and the two roads contradict each other.

At the end of October 2025, at the Hukou National Review Ground, a row of M1A2T "Abrams" tanks sat on the concrete. Lai Ching-te stepped down from the reviewing stand to preside over the commissioning ceremony, announcing that "the world's most powerful tanks" had officially joined the armed forces' combat order. The first batch of 38 arrived in Taiwan in December 2024; the second batch of 42 arrived in July 2025; the final 28 were delivered in the first quarter of 2026 — 108 tanks in total, deployed to the 584th Armored Brigade and the 269th Mechanized Brigade.[^1]

At the same moment, former Chief of General Staff Lee Hsi-min was signing books at a bookstore. His book _Taiwan's Win_, which he had written, argues that Taiwan should not buy expensive large weapons and instead should invest in large quantities of cheap, mobile, concealable small systems. When someone asked him how he viewed the Ministry of National Defense's rationale of "beach-landing decisive battle" for purchasing the M1A2T tanks, he smiled wryly: "When I see this, I can only smile wryly. If a tank can be an asymmetric weapon, then what isn't an asymmetric weapon?"[^2]

That sentence is the most honest key for understanding Taiwan's defense in 2026. The government talks about the Porcupine Strategy while signing contracts for America's most expensive weapons; on one hand it proclaims defense indigenization, while 90% of the budget goes to American suppliers; it extends conscription while acknowledging that 200,000 drones are the real future combat force. These contradictions are not oversights — they are an island being pulled back and forth between two survival logics. It wants to become a porcupine, but its body still remembers being a leopard.

## Why a Porcupine

The term "Porcupine Strategy" was coined by William Murray, a professor at the U.S. Naval War College, in a 2008 paper. His argument was simple: Taiwan cannot win against the PLA in conventional military power; any warship costing tens of billions, any expensive jet, might survive only hours after hostilities begin. The only rational approach is to buy large quantities of cheap land-based mobile missiles, mines, and air defense systems — making Taiwan a "hedgehog not worth eating."[^3]

This concept was not immediately adopted in Taiwan. The person who actually formalized it as military strategy was former Chief of General Staff Lee Hsi-min. In 2017, during his tenure, Lee Hsi-min completed an internal document that the military called the "Overall Defense Concept" (ODC). Its core was a single question: if the PLA actually attacks, how does Taiwan make itself "not a good meal"? Lee Hsi-min's answer was mines, mobile missile vehicles, shoulder-launched guided missiles, and large quantities of drones — cheap, numerous, concealable, hit-and-run.[^4]

His logic was the same as Murray's: Taiwan does not need to win; it only needs to make the cost of invasion high enough that the other side won't risk it.

But ODC was shelved after Lee Hsi-min retired in 2019. The Ministry of National Defense purchased more jets, more tanks, and continued pushing the indigenous submarine program. When journalists asked Lee Hsi-min how he viewed this, he said President Tsai Ing-wen was "the most respectful president toward soldiers I have ever seen," but he added: "Whether she understands ODC — I'm not sure, because different people may interpret it differently."[^5]

> **📝 Curator's note**
> ODC's counter-intuitiveness lies in how it challenges the entire military's self-identity. For career officers, "jets, warships, tanks" are the cultural pillars of their service branches — buying drones is tantamount to acknowledging that the army, navy, and air force have all become "secondary" roles. The reason ODC was shelved after Lee Hsi-min retired was partly strategic disagreement, partly institutional survival instinct.

## Four Months to One Year

Taiwan's conscription system is the thermometer of the island's security anxiety.

In the 1950s, mandatory service was three years. At the Cold War's peak, Taiwan maintained 600,000 active troops, with men inducted upon adulthood. As the Cold War ended in the 1990s, the period began to shrink. The 2000s saw a push toward a "hybrid volunteer-conscript" system, and in 2013 mandatory service was cut to four months — which military commentators derisively called "summer camp."[^6]

The turning point was the Russia-Ukraine War of February 2022. A country expected to surrender within three days held on using drones, portable Stinger missiles, and total national mobilization — and within six months had driven Russian forces back from Kyiv. The resonance this war triggered in Taiwanese society was beyond description. Suddenly everyone was asking a question they had previously not dared to ask: what if it were us?

On December 27, 2022, Tsai Ing-wen stood at a press conference and announced that mandatory service would be restored to one year, effective January 2024. She called it "a difficult but necessary decision." Monthly pay for conscripts was raised from approximately NT$6,500 to NT$26,307.[^7]

The training content for the new one-year service was also revamped: 8 weeks basic training, 18 weeks garrison training, 7 weeks specialty training, 13 weeks base training, 6 weeks combined exercises — with rifle live-fire of no fewer than 800 rounds. Compared to the old four-month system's stereotype of "standing guard and sweeping floors," this was a backbone reconstruction.

> **⚠️ Contested viewpoint**
> Polling support for restoring one-year conscription exceeded 70%, but the response among the young generation actually facing induction was mixed. VOA interviewed multiple draft-age men: some frankly admitted "fear of war," some questioned whether one year of training could truly produce combat capability, and some said outright "I don't want to give my life for politicians' mistakes." Supporters called it the minimum national defense obligation; critics said one year is still not enough. One-year conscription is both consensus and compromise — nobody is truly satisfied, but most accept it as the furthest anyone can go right now.[^8]

## The Hai Kun's 147 Days

Taiwan has long relied on American arms sales. F-16 fighters, Patriot missiles, M1A2 tanks, Harpoon missiles — almost all heavy equipment comes from Washington. This ties Taiwan's defense to American political will: when the White House changes occupants, the pace of arms sales can change.

"Defense indigenization" therefore became a long-term goal, with the indigenous submarine program as the flagship project. On September 28, 2023, the first domestically built submarine, the Hai Kun (hull number 711), was launched at CSBC Corporation's Kaohsiung shipyard. The submarine is 70 meters long with a displacement of approximately 2,500 tons, using an X-shaped tail fin design. Tsai Ing-wen said that day: "History will remember this day." The plan calls for building seven more identical submarines.[^9]

But what history may remember is not the 2023 launching ceremony, but the delays that followed over two years. The handover timeline originally planned for 2024 was pushed back; on June 17, 2025, the Hai Kun finally began sea trials — first surface navigation tests, then shallow diving, and eventually deep diving. By November 28, 2025, the Hai Kun ended a 147-day period without going to sea, returning to the water for surface navigation verification — with several critical steps still remaining before completing diving tests.[^10]

When Minister of National Defense Wellington Koo was asked about the Hai Kun's timeline, he gave an answer military brass rarely speak aloud: "Other countries typically take more than 7 years to build a new domestically-produced submarine — some even 16 years. Our country initially set a 5-year target, which was overly optimistic, failed to understand reality, and especially failed to consider national conditions."[^11]

Koo did not set a delivery timeline. He only said: "Only when testing is complete and safety is confirmed will the subsequent delivery procedure proceed."

> **✦** "History will remember this day." These were Tsai Ing-wen's exact words at the Hai Kun launching ceremony on September 28, 2023. Two years later, the Hai Kun had not yet completed diving tests. History will remember that day — but not necessarily in the way the person who said those words intended.

## Between "The Strongest Tank" and "The Cheapest Drone"

In November 2025, the Lai Ching-te government proposed the _Special Act to Strengthen National Defense Resilience and Procure Asymmetric Combat Capabilities_, planning to allocate NT$1.25 trillion (approximately US$40 billion) in special defense budget over 8 years. If passed, Taiwan's defense spending would rise from the current 2.5% of GDP to 3.3%. Of that amount, approximately NT$950 billion (76%) would flow to American arms manufacturers for traditional weapons platforms.[^12]

The other half of the same budget's picture: the plan calls for purchasing over 200,000 various drones and more than 1,000 unmanned surface vessels, covering multi-rotor reconnaissance drones, fixed-wing attack aircraft, and suicide drones. Approximately NT$300 billion will be manufactured in Taiwan domestically, building what is called a "non-red supply chain" — a defense industrial system excluding Chinese components.[^13]

These two halves of the picture placed together constitute the paradox behind Lee Hsi-min's wry smile: the Porcupine Strategy has been verbally accepted, but the budget still tilts toward traditional large weapons.

Meanwhile, three names have emerged in the National Chung-Shan Institute of Science and Technology's indigenous drone program, representing Taiwan's attempt to localize the "Ukraine experience":

- **Chien Hsiang**: Anti-radiation suicide drone; mass production began in 2023 with 104 aircraft to be produced for the air force over six years. With radar electronic parameter analysis capability, it can detect enemy air defense radar signals and then conduct high-speed "suicide attacks" — similar in concept to Israel's Harpy.[^14]
- **Rui Yuan II**: Medium-long range reconnaissance drone; wingspan 12 meters, guidance range 300 km, maximum range 2,000 km; an "armed attack variant" is under development.
- **Teng Yun II**: MALE (Medium-Altitude Long-Endurance) large drone using the same engine as the U.S. military's MQ-9B, airborne for more than 20 hours; not yet in mass production.

None of the three is cheap, and none counts as "large quantities." The real 200,000 aircraft will probably be smaller, cheaper, possibly even 3D-printed tactical drones. On the Ukrainian battlefield, a drone costing tens of thousands of dollars can destroy a tank worth hundreds of millions — this equation has changed the calculus of every modern military.

But the fate of the NT$1.25 trillion budget is not a strategic question — it is a political one. Since December 2, 2025, the Legislative Yuan under opposition party control has blocked the special budget at least eight times. The opposition questions the budget compilation method and oversight mechanisms; the ruling party accuses the opposition of blocking national defense by blocking the budget. American congressional delegations visiting Taiwan publicly called on the Legislative Yuan to pass the budget, with the U.S. representative issuing a rare direct statement of support.[^15]

This Legislative Yuan standoff is actually the third level of Taiwan's defense contradictions: whether to spend this money, and how to spend it, is itself the most sensitive question in cross-strait relations.

> **📝 Curator's note**
> The defense budget is a mirror reflecting not military strength but political will. Many countries have money to buy weapons; very few are willing to bear the political cost of buying them. Taiwan is simultaneously both kinds of country — wealthy and divided. The NT$1.25 trillion's real stake is not what gets purchased, but whether it can politically pass at all.

## Black Bear Academy: Chen Shui-bian's Open Letter

The deepest transformation in Taiwan's defense is not in the weapons list — it is in conversations in living rooms and coffee shops.

"Black Bear Academy" was founded in 2021 by Shen Po-yang, a criminology professor at National Taipei University, and Ho Cheng-hui, a veteran civil defense researcher. It focuses on "total defense" education. Its basic training camp has four classes: contemporary military science to dispel military rumors; information literacy and cognitive warfare to counter disinformation; emergency first aid, hemostasis, and casualty transport; and evacuation preparation planning.[^16]

In 2022, semiconductor entrepreneur Tsao Hsing-cheng announced a donation of NT$600 million to fund Black Bear Academy. On January 7, 2025, former President Chen Shui-bian publicly disclosed online that Tsao had fulfilled this pledge, and revealed that the two had set a target of "training 3 million Black Bear Warriors within 3 years" — representing about one-third of Taiwan's 9 million households, with the hope that at least one person in each household would have basic defense knowledge.[^17]

Black Bear Academy's courses were full from the moment they opened. A female student told _Global Views_ magazine in an interview: "I don't want to fight in a war — I just don't want to be unable to even protect my children when war comes." This sentence later became Black Bear Academy's own promotional copy.[^18]

> **✦** "I don't want to fight in a war — I just don't want to be unable to even protect my children when war comes." This is the most honest sentence about Taiwan's 2026 defense consciousness. It is not patriotism — it is the most primal family instinct.

The Total Defense Mobilization Preparedness Act simultaneously established a reserve mobilization system. After 2022, reserve recall exercises expanded from 5 days at a time to 14 days, with greatly increased training intensity. Official estimates put mobilizable reservists at over 2 million — combined with 180,000 active-duty troops, this means that in wartime Taiwan could theoretically mobilize armed personnel approaching one-tenth of the labor force.

## Han Kuang 41: Ten Days and Nine Nights of In-Depth Defense

At dawn on July 9, 2025, Han Kuang Exercise 41 launched simultaneously across Taiwan. At 10 days and 9 nights, it was the longest in the history of the Han Kuang exercises since their 1984 founding, mobilizing 20,000 reservists — also the largest ever.[^19]

The biggest difference between Han Kuang 41 and past exercises was the emotional tone of the scenario. Past exercises tended to showcase military might: tanks in formation, fighters streaking overhead, naval fleets in formation. The core assumption of Han Kuang 41 was stark: **the armed forces in wartime will hold only partial air superiority and must fight a prolonged war**.

The exercise was divided into six phases:

1. **Days 1–3**: Chinese gray-zone harassment; Taiwan enters "routine crisis management." This was a lesson the Russia-Ukraine War taught Taiwan — war doesn't start with tanks crossing borders; it starts with fishing boat harassment, communication jamming, and disinformation floods.
2. **Day 4**: Wartime preparation deployment, moving troops and establishing positions.
3. **Days 5–10**: Active combat, subdivided into four sub-phases: anti-landing, coastal combat, in-depth defense, and sustained combat.

The final three sub-phases — coastal combat, in-depth defense, and sustained combat — are what was genuinely new in Han Kuang 41. It explicitly acknowledges: active-duty troops cannot hold the entire depth; reservists must go to the second line; conscripts and reserve forces must hold down the enemy that has broken through, allowing the main combat forces composed of volunteer soldiers to find openings for counterattack.

This is the shadow of ODC. Lee Hsi-min's original concept was to turn Taiwan into a "net" — if the front can't hold, withdraw into depth; wherever you withdraw to, make the enemy pay a price. Han Kuang 41 is the first time this concept was systematically adopted in an exercise scenario.[^20]

## 160 Kilometers and One Bet

The Taiwan Strait is approximately 130 km at its narrowest and approximately 400 km at its widest. This body of water is described in military scholarship as a natural barrier for "anti-access/area denial" (A2/AD). For the PLA to cross the Taiwan Strait, it needs not just ships and planes but continuous sea and air superiority over several weeks along with amphibious landing capability — something only two or three of the world's existing militaries can accomplish.[^21]

Taiwan's defense strategy rests on a cold calculation: **there is no need to win outright; only to hold out until international intervention**. A porcupine doesn't need to defeat the hunter — it only needs to make the hunter feel "this one isn't worth eating." The M1A2T tanks, F-16V fighters, Hai Kun submarine — their mission is not to defeat the PLA; their mission is to buy time. Two hundred thousand drones, 20,000 reservists, 3 million Black Bear Warriors — all are doing the same thing.

But the premise of this calculation is: the international community will intervene.

The F-16V Block 70, originally scheduled for delivery in 2024, was delayed to 2027 onward due to American supply chain and software problems.[^22] The Hai Kun's time from launching to projected delivery stretched by over two years. The NT$1.25 trillion special budget was blocked eight times in the Legislative Yuan. The M1A2T tanks arrived on schedule — but they are the kind of weapon Lee Hsi-min thinks about when he says "how can a tank be an asymmetric weapon?" Every timeline, every budget line, every piece of equipment, is a reminder of the same thing: Taiwan's defense is not Taiwan's plan alone.
