> **30-Second Overview:** On September 28, 2023, Taiwan's first domestically built submarine, the Hai Kun, was launched in Kaohsiung. President Tsai Ing-wen declared the day would be remembered by history. Starting in 2024, mandatory military service was extended from four months back to one year. By late 2025, the Executive Yuan proposed a NT$1.25 trillion special budget to acquire 200,000 drones and over a thousand unmanned vessels. Taiwan's defense is shifting from "buying weapons" to "rebuilding the system" — from battleships and big guns to a landscape bristling with quills. In military doctrine, this is called the Porcupine Strategy.

In 2017, Admiral Lee Hsi-min, then Taiwan's Chief of the General Staff, championed a classified framework before his retirement. It had no public name; internally, the military called it the Overall Defense Concept (ODC). It boiled down to one question: if the People's Liberation Army actually came, how could Taiwan make itself too costly to consume?[^1]

Lee's answer wasn't more fighter jets and destroyers. It was sea mines, drones, mobile missile launchers, and shoulder-fired missiles. Cheap, numerous, concealable, shoot-and-scoot. The logic was straightforward: Taiwan doesn't need to win. It just needs to make the price of invasion too steep for anyone to gamble on.

William Murray, a professor at the U.S. Naval War College, had laid the theoretical groundwork for this idea as early as 2008, coining it the "Porcupine Strategy": instead of spending on expensive fighter jets and warships, invest in large quantities of cheap, land-based mobile missiles, sea mines, and air defense systems — turning Taiwan into a porcupine covered in quills[^2].

> **Curator's Note**
> The counterintuitive truth of the Porcupine Strategy is this: the most expensive weapons you buy may be the first ones destroyed. A warship worth billions might survive only hours once a Taiwan Strait conflict begins. A mobile missile launcher hidden under a highway overpass? The enemy might never find it at all.

## From Three Years to Four Months to One Year

Taiwan's conscription policy is a barometer of the island's security anxiety.

In the 1950s, mandatory service lasted three years. At the peak of the Cold War, Taiwan maintained a standing army of 600,000, and every man enlisted upon reaching adulthood. After the Cold War ended in the 1990s, service was gradually shortened. The 2000s brought a shift toward an all-volunteer force supplemented by conscripts, and by 2013, mandatory service had been cut to just four months — a stint that military commentators mockingly called "summer camp."[^3]

The turning point came in 2022. Russia's invasion of Ukraine forced the world to confront the reality of conventional war again. Public attitudes toward defense in Taiwan began to shift. On December 27, 2022, President Tsai Ing-wen announced that mandatory service would be restored to one year, effective January 2024. Monthly pay for conscripts was raised from roughly NT$6,500 to NT$26,307.[^4]

The training itself was overhauled: 8 weeks of basic training, 18 weeks at garrison, 7 weeks of specialization, 13 weeks of field exercises, and 6 weeks of joint operations drills. Riflemen fire no fewer than 800 live rounds. Compared to the old four-month regime — widely dismissed as "standing guard and sweeping floors" — this represents a fundamental rebuild.

> **Contested Viewpoint**
> Polls show over 70% support for the return of one-year service, but the reaction among young men actually facing conscription is more complicated. In VOA interviews, several draft-age men admitted to feeling reluctant about combat, while others questioned whether a single year of training could truly produce combat-ready soldiers. Supporters argue it's the bare minimum for national defense; critics say even a year isn't enough.

## Defense Autonomy: The Hai Kun and the Brave Eagle

For decades, Taiwan's arsenal depended almost entirely on U.S. arms sales. F-16 fighters, Patriot missiles, M1A2 tanks — virtually all major equipment came from Washington. This tied Taiwan's security to America's political will: a change in the White House could mean a change in the pace and scope of weapons deliveries.[^5]

"Defense autonomy" thus became a long-term ambition. Two flagship programs stand out.

**Indigenous Defense Submarine (IDS):** On September 28, 2023, the first domestically built submarine, Hai Kun (hull number 711), was launched at CSBC Corporation's Kaohsiung shipyard. At 70 meters long with a displacement of roughly 2,500 tons and an X-form stern rudder, the Hai Kun entered sea trials when it sailed out of Kaohsiung harbor for the first time in June 2025. Seven more submarines of the same class are planned.[^6]

**Advanced Jet Trainer (AJT):** The Brave Eagle, developed by AIDC (Aerospace Industrial Development Corporation), has delivered 19 units to date. It replaces the AT-3 and F-5 aircraft, both of which had been in training service for over half a century.

> **Highlight** — "The Hai Kun isn't just a submarine. It's the first time Taiwan proved it could build a weapon that keeps the other side up at night."

## A NT$1.25 Trillion Bet

In November 2025, the Lai Ching-te administration introduced the Special Act for Strengthening National Defense Resilience and Asymmetric Capabilities, proposing a special defense budget of NT$1.25 trillion (roughly US$40 billion) over eight years. If passed, Taiwan's defense spending would rise to 3.3% of GDP.[^7]

Among the seven major line items, drones command the most attention. The plan calls for over 200,000 drones of various types and more than a thousand unmanned vessels, spanning multi-rotor reconnaissance drones, fixed-wing attack drones, and loitering munitions. Approximately NT$300 billion is earmarked for domestic production, building what officials call a "non-red supply chain" — a defense industrial base that excludes Chinese components entirely.[^8]

The catalyst was Ukraine. On the battlefields there, an FPV drone costing a few tens of thousands of NT dollars can destroy a tank worth hundreds of millions. In a Taiwan Strait conflict, 200,000 drones scattered along Taiwan's western coastline would be like scattering spikes across every beach.

> **Curator's Note**
> The NT$1.25 trillion budget currently faces resistance in the Legislative Yuan. Opposition parties have challenged the budgeting methodology and oversight mechanisms. The American representative in Taipei publicly urged legislators to pass it. The fate of this budget is not just a defense question — it's a microcosm of Taiwan's domestic political struggles.

## Whole-of-Society Defense: Not Just the Military's Job

The deepest shift in Taiwan's defense isn't about weapons. It's about mindset.

The All-Out Defense Mobilization Readiness Act established a reserve mobilization system capable of calling up over two million reservists. After 2022, reserve training was expanded from five-day refreshers to fourteen-day sessions, with a significant increase in rigor.

Civilians are joining the effort too. Organizations like the Kuma Academy (named after the Formosan black bear) offer courses in first aid, emergency communications, and urban survival. Enrollment surged after 2022. The concept of "whole-of-society defense" has evolved from military jargon into a social movement.[^9]

| Cold War Era                     | 2026                                          |
| -------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| 600,000 active troops            | ~180,000 active + 2 million reserves          |
| Three-year conscription          | One-year conscription + volunteer core        |
| Head-on decisive battle doctrine | Asymmetric warfare + whole-of-society defense |
| Weapons entirely from U.S. aid   | Defense autonomy + U.S.-Taiwan co-production  |
| Battleships and big guns         | Drones + mobile missiles + sea mines          |

## 160 Kilometers of Water

The Taiwan Strait is roughly 130 kilometers wide at its narrowest point and 400 kilometers at its widest. In military terms, this body of water is a natural Anti-Access/Area Denial (A2/AD) barrier. To cross it, the PLA would need not just ships and aircraft, but sustained air and naval superiority over several weeks along with full amphibious landing capability.

Taiwan's defense strategy rests on a cold calculus: it doesn't need to win — it just needs to hold out until the international community intervenes. A porcupine doesn't have to defeat the predator. It just has to make the predator decide the meal isn't worth the pain.[^10]

But this calculus has a premise: that the world will actually intervene. And that's a guarantee no one has ever been willing to make.

> **Highlight** — "Taiwan's defense is not a military problem. It's a question of confidence — whether you believe you're worth protecting, and whether anyone else will show up to protect you."
