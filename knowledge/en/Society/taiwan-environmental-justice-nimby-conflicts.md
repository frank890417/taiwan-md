# Taiwan Environmental Justice and NIMBY Conflicts

Environmental Justice (EJ) concerns the fair distribution of environmental burdens and benefits. In Taiwan, environmental problems generated by industrial development tend to concentrate in specific regions and specific groups, forming a phenomenon of "environmental inequality." From garbage incinerators and nuclear waste disposal to petrochemical industrial parks, these necessary but risky facilities consistently trigger NIMBY (Not In My Back Yard) conflicts, reflecting deep contradictions in Taiwan's distribution of environmental risk.

## Theoretical Foundations of Environmental Justice

### Unequal Distribution of Environmental Burdens

The core concept of environmental justice is "distributive fairness." Ideally, environmental benefits (clean air, beautiful scenery) and environmental burdens (polluting facilities, health risks) should be evenly distributed. But in reality, environmental burdens tend to concentrate in specific areas — usually economically disadvantaged communities with little political influence.

Taiwan's environmental burden distribution is markedly uneven. The Sixth Naphtha Cracker (六輕) petrochemical complex is in Mailiao Township, Yunlin County, where the local economy is mostly farming and fishing and resident incomes are below average. The Kaohsiung Linhai Industrial Zone is in Xiaogang District, an area with high population density but limited political influence. This distribution pattern reflects the "principle of least resistance" — polluting facilities tend to be sited where opposition is weakest.

### Procedural Justice and Participation Rights

Beyond distributive fairness, environmental justice also emphasizes "procedural justice" — that all affected groups should have equal opportunity to participate in environmental decisions. But Taiwan's environmental impact assessment (EIA) system has long emphasized technical evaluation while neglecting public participation.

Residents often only learn of facilities after they've been confirmed for siting, lacking the opportunity to participate in advance decisions. Even with public hearings, they often become formalities; public opinion struggles to influence outcomes. This "decide first, then communicate" pattern is a major reason for NIMBY conflicts.

### Intergenerational Justice and Risk Bearing

Environmental justice also involves intergenerational fairness. Nuclear waste has half-lives of tens of thousands of years; the current generation enjoys the benefits of nuclear power generation, but future generations bear the risks. Likewise, industrial development brings economic growth, but environmental costs may extend for decades.

Indigenous traditional territories often become disposal sites for waste — such as Lanyu's nuclear waste storage and mountain townships' garbage landfills. These decisions were often made in eras when Indigenous peoples had no political voice, but the impacts continue to this day, forming a "colonial environmentalism."

## NIMBY Dilemmas in Garbage Disposal

### Incinerator Siting Disputes

The construction process of Taiwan's garbage incinerators has been filled with NIMBY protests. When the "one incinerator per county/city" policy was promoted in the 1990s, almost every incinerator faced strong opposition. The Xindian Ankeng incinerator protests were the most intense, with residents forming a self-help committee for a decade-long opposition movement.

Reasons for opposition included health risks, declining property values, and reduced quality of life. Dioxin emissions were the biggest concern; though new-generation incinerators have reduced dioxin emissions to extremely low levels, public fear of "toxins" is hard to dispel. The "not in my backyard" mentality is understandable, but garbage has to go somewhere.

The government has tried to use feedback funds to resolve disputes. Townships hosting incinerators receive feedback funds of tens of dollars per ton of waste, used for local development. But monetary compensation cannot fully resolve concerns about environmental risk, and the fairness of feedback mechanisms has been questioned.

### Cross-Municipal Garbage Disposal

Taipei's garbage is sent to New Taipei for disposal, New Taipei's to Taoyuan, forming a "garbage tour" phenomenon. While this cross-regional disposal solves the immediate needs of each municipality, it raises new fairness issues.

Remote mountain landfills become the final destination for urban garbage. Mountain townships such as Wulai, Sanxia, and Shiding — sparsely populated and politically weak — often become designated landfill sites. Local residents protest: "Why should we bear what the city people produce?"

The distribution of garbage disposal fees is also unfair. Garbage-producing municipalities pay disposal fees, but the compensation received by municipalities bearing the environmental risk is often disproportionate. The "polluter pays" principle is hard to realize in cross-municipal garbage disposal.

### Class Differences in Recycling

Garbage reduction policies affect different socioeconomic classes differently. Middle- and high-income households can buy better-packaged products with less waste; low-income households often buy cheap goods with more packaging and more waste.

The burden of recycling is also uneven. Street scavengers are mostly economically vulnerable elderly people doing dangerous, dirty recycling work for meager income. Middle- and high-income households enjoy clean environments while the bottom of society bears the labor cost of recycling.

## Ethnic Disputes in Nuclear Waste Disposal

### The Lanyu Nuclear Waste Incident

The Lanyu nuclear waste storage facility is a classic case of Taiwan's environmental justice disputes. In 1982, Taipower set up a low-level radioactive waste storage facility on Lanyu without sufficient prior disclosure of radiation risks to the Tao Indigenous residents. It was claimed at the time to be a "fish cannery"; the Tao people received the nuclear waste without knowing.

This incident highlighted multiple injustices: ethnic injustice (exploiting the disadvantaged position of an Indigenous group), intergenerational injustice (nuclear waste impacts continuing for centuries), informational injustice (concealing real risk), procedural injustice (lacking real informed consent).

The Tao people's protests have lasted forty years. They demand the nuclear waste be moved out, restoring the land of the ancestral spirits. "Nuclear waste out of Lanyu" is not just an environmental demand but an assertion of Indigenous self-determination. Though the government has promised removal, the final disposal site has been hard to find, and the nuclear waste remains on Lanyu.

### Final Disposal Site Siting Difficulties

High-level nuclear waste requires geologically stable deep geological disposal. Taipower commissioned Sinotech Engineering Consultants to conduct geological surveys; Jinshan, Wanli, and Penghu were all on the candidate list, but all faced strong opposition.

Public fear of nuclear waste is understandable, but the reality of "someone has to bear it" cannot be evaded. Sweden, Finland, and other countries solve siting through volunteer communities, generous feedback, and transparent decision-making, but Taiwanese society has weak foundations of trust, making this model hard to replicate.

The screening criteria for candidate sites also raise disputes. Technical safety is important, but social acceptance is equally non-negligible. Selecting sites purely by geological conditions while ignoring local public opinion will inevitably trigger protests. How to balance technical and social rationality is a policy-making challenge.

### Fair Burden of Nuclear Decommissioning

Taiwan's three nuclear power plants are being decommissioned successively, generating large amounts of low-level radioactive waste. Disposing of these waste materials also faces environmental justice issues. The nuclear plants are mostly in the north, with electricity primarily supplied to Taipei and New Taipei urban areas, but the waste disposal sites may be set in other counties.

Decommissioning costs are borne by all citizens, but environmental risks concentrate in specific areas. This pattern of socialized cost and localized risk is the embodiment of nuclear policy environmental injustice. How to make beneficiaries bear corresponding responsibility requires institutional design innovation.

## Environmental Burden of Petrochemicals

### Health Risks of the Sixth Naphtha Cracker

The Sixth Naphtha Cracker petrochemical complex in Mailiao, Yunlin, is Taiwan's largest petrochemical base, with annual output value exceeding NT$2 trillion. But the complex also brings serious environmental burdens: air pollution, water pollution, soil pollution, health risks.

Research by the National Taiwan University College of Public Health found that cancer incidence rates among residents near the Sixth Naphtha Cracker are significantly higher. Lung cancer and liver cancer rates are 20–30% higher than the national average. Asthma and allergy rates among children are also significantly elevated. These health risks are mainly borne by local residents, but the economic benefits go mainly to corporations and shareholders.

The Sixth Naphtha Cracker's air pollution affects a wide area. Pollutants such as PM2.5, sulfur dioxide, and nitrogen oxides spread on the wind, affecting Changhua, Nantou, and other neighboring counties. EPA air quality monitoring shows that the air pollution concentration in Yunlin region has long exceeded standards.

### Dalinpu Relocation Disputes

Kaohsiung's Dalinpu area is surrounded by petrochemical industrial parks, with residents long exposed to air pollution. The government is pushing the Dalinpu relocation plan, but progress is slow, causing resident dissatisfaction.

Relocation compensation standards are the focus of dispute. The government acquires homes at market price, but residents believe the compensation is insufficient to buy equivalent housing elsewhere. Elderly residents are reluctant to leave their hometown; younger people worry about employment. Relocation involves rebuilding the entire community, not just buying and selling houses.

Living arrangements during temporary relocation are also a problem. Children's schooling, elder care, work commutes all need to be rearranged. The government's resettlement plan lacks detail, and residents lack certainty about their future lives.

### Environmental Monitoring Transparency

Petrochemical industry environmental monitoring is often conducted by the companies themselves, and the credibility of monitoring results is questioned. The public demands the government set up independent monitoring stations, but the technical threshold for interpreting monitoring data is high, making it hard for ordinary citizens to judge.

The follow-up monitoring mechanism for environmental impact assessment is weak. Many EIA commitments at industrial zones are hard to verify after the fact, and violation penalties lack force. The public lacks confidence in government oversight ability, deepening distrust of industrial facilities.

Citizen monitoring movements have arisen. Organizations such as the Changhua Environmental Protection Union and the Yunlin Environmental Protection Union purchase their own monitoring equipment to conduct air quality monitoring. This citizen science compensates for the inadequacy of official monitoring, but also reflects the government's regulatory shortcomings.

## Social Distribution of Environmental Risk

### Class and Environmental Exposure

Taiwan's environmental risk is clearly correlated with social class. Housing near industrial zones is mostly inhabited by low- and middle-income families because of lower house prices and cheaper rents. High-income families live in better-environment areas, such as hillside residential areas and ocean-view villas.

Occupational environmental exposure also has class differences. Factory workers, construction workers, and sanitation workers — blue-collar workers — are exposed to higher environmental risks. White-collar workers in air-conditioned offices have relatively lower environmental exposure.

Education level affects environmental risk awareness. Higher-educated citizens better understand environmental risks and are more capable of taking protective measures. Lower-educated citizens may underestimate risks, or be unable to move out of high-risk areas due to economic considerations.

### Urban-Rural Environmental Gap

Urban areas enjoy better environmental quality and public services. Public transportation is well-developed, green spaces are abundant, medical resources are sufficient. Rural areas often bear environmental burdens generated by cities: garbage landfills, sewage treatment plants, and thermal power plants are mostly in rural areas.

Rural environmental monitoring networks are sparser, and pollution incidents are harder to detect in time. Medical resources are insufficient, making environmental health risks hard to handle promptly. Farmers may continue cultivating polluted land due to economic considerations.

Environmental justice issues during the urbanization process also deserve attention. Urban renewal often relocates low-income residents to areas with worse environments. Soil pollution remediation at industrial sites is often borne by the public sector, while land appreciation gains are obtained by private parties.

### Ethnicity and Environmental Justice

Indigenous areas often become designated locations for environmental risk facilities. Mountain township garbage landfills, nuclear waste storage facilities, and large reservoirs all impact Indigenous traditional territories.

Water resource distribution also has ethnic differences. Indigenous communities in mountain townships often lack safe drinking water, while downstream urban areas have no water shortage. The benefits and costs of water resources are disproportionate.

Indigenous environmental knowledge and management systems have long been ignored. Traditional natural resource management methods may be more sustainable than modern scientific management, but lack policy attention.

## Response Strategies and Institutional Reform

### EIA System Reform

The current EIA system emphasizes technology over society and needs to increase the weight of social impact assessment. Assessment scope should include health risks, social impacts, and cultural impacts. The assessment process should have more public participation opportunities.

The composition of EIA committees needs to be diversified. In addition to technical experts, social science experts, local representatives, and NGO representatives should be included. Decision-making should be more transparent, and public opinion should have clear handling procedures.

The follow-up monitoring mechanism of EIA needs strengthening. Set up independent third-party monitoring agencies to regularly review the implementation of EIA commitments. Penalties for violations should have deterrent effect and not be merely symbolic fines.

### Risk Communication Mechanisms

Establish effective risk communication mechanisms allowing the public to understand and participate in risk decisions. Risk information should be presented in ways the public can understand, avoiding technical black boxes.

Set up permanent communication platforms, communicating not only when facilities are being built. Hold community meetings regularly to collect public opinion and respond to public concerns. Building trust requires long-term effort.

Media play an important role in risk communication. Science journalists need better professional training to accurately report environmental risk information. Avoid sensational reporting, but also do not ignore reasonable public concerns.

### Compensation and Feedback Mechanisms

Design fair compensation mechanisms — not just monetary compensation, but also community development needs. The use of feedback funds should be transparent and public, used to actually improve local quality of life.

Establish a health fund system to provide health monitoring and medical security for residents near environmental risk facilities. Regular health checks, medical fee subsidies, and health risk research can all be included in the fund's scope.

Consider establishing an "Environmental Justice Fund" to help environmentally disadvantaged communities improve environmental quality. Funding sources may include pollution fees, environmental taxes, carbon taxes, and other environmental-related revenues.

### Civic Participation Mechanisms

Strengthen the legal foundation for civic participation in environmental decision-making. Establish citizens' rights to environmental information, participation, and oversight. Government information should be proactively disclosed, and public opinion should have clear handling procedures.

Empower environmentally disadvantaged communities to participate. Provide legal assistance, technical support, organizational training, and other resources, allowing disadvantaged communities to effectively participate in environmental decisions.

Promote the application of participatory budgeting in environmental policy. Let citizens directly participate in environmental budget allocation decisions, increasing the democratic legitimacy of policies.

## International Experience and Lessons

### US Environmental Justice Movement

The US environmental justice movement originated in the 1980s, focusing on the concentration of toxic waste facilities in African American communities. After thirty years of development, environmental justice has been integrated into the mainstream of federal policy.

The US EPA has set up an Office of Environmental Justice to ensure all policies consider environmental justice aspects. Federal agencies must assess impacts on disadvantaged communities when formulating policies.

California's Environmental Justice Act (SB 535) requires that 25% of greenhouse gas reduction investments must be used for disadvantaged communities. This "beneficiary-first" principle is worth Taiwan's reference.

### Procedural Justice in Europe

The European Aarhus Convention establishes procedural rights such as the right to environmental information, public participation, and judicial relief. Member states must establish corresponding legal systems.

Sweden's environmental court system enables citizens to effectively seek environmental rights remedies. Professional judges, technical experts, and civilian representatives jointly hear environmental cases. Verdicts have binding force, and both governments and corporations must comply.

The Netherlands' EIA system emphasizes public participation. The public can not only express opinions but also influence assessment scope and methods. Assessment results must respond to issues of public concern.

### Japan's Risk Communication

After the nuclear disaster, Japan vigorously promoted risk communication systems. The government set up dedicated agencies, trained risk communication experts, and established standardized communication procedures.

The Fukushima nuclear disaster handling experience shows that risk communication is not just a technical issue but more of a trust issue. The government must acknowledge uncertainty and bear decision-making responsibility to build public trust.

Local governments in Japan play an important role in risk communication. The attitudes and policies of local heads often determine the success or failure of risk communication.

## Outlook and Conclusion

Taiwan's environmental justice issues reflect deeper structures of social inequality. Solving environmental justice issues requires simultaneously addressing multiple inequalities — class, ethnicity, urban-rural, intergenerational.

Future challenges include: climate change exacerbating unequal distribution of environmental risks, just transition during the energy transformation, social impacts of circular economy development, environmental justice issues in international trade.

Environmental justice is not just an ideal but a practice. Establishing fair, transparent, participatory environmental governance systems, allowing all citizens to enjoy environmental quality and bear environmental responsibility — this is a necessary condition for Taiwan to move toward a sustainable society.

Environmental decision-making in a democratic society should be based on adequate information, open debate, and procedural justice. There are no perfect solutions, but more equitable processes can be pursued. Realizing environmental justice requires joint efforts of government, business, and civil society — and active participation of every citizen.
