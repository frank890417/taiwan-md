Late on the night of October 25, 1971, in the United Nations General Assembly Hall in New York. After Foreign Minister Chou Shu-kai of the Republic of China finished reading the statement, he turned and stepped down from the green marble podium; the entire delegation followed him out. From entering to leaving, it took less than 20 minutes[^1]. Half an hour later, Resolution 2758 was passed by a vote of 76 to 35. The "representatives of Chiang Kai-shek" were expelled, and the People's Republic of China officially took its seat for China. Taiwan went from being a founding member of the United Nations (on August 24, 1945, Chiang Kai-shek signed the instrument of ratification for the UN Charter in Chongqing[^2]) to an outsider of international organizations overnight.

> **Understand in 30 seconds**: On the surface, this was the tragic exit of the Republic of China based on the principle of "neither Han nor bandit can coexist"; in reality, Chiang Kai-shek had already accepted a compromise of "two Chinas" as early as early 1971, and the U.S. State Department even praised Taiwan for showing "extraordinary flexibility"[^3]. The real key was Henry Kissinger's secret visit to Beijing in July of the same year. While officials in Taipei and Washington were still in the dark, Kissinger had already revealed his hand to Zhou Enlai: the United States would not sincerely fight to keep Taiwan in the United Nations. Half a century later, the U.S., UK, EU, Australia, and the Netherlands have successively stated that the text of Resolution 2758 does not contain the word "Taiwan" at all.

## From Founding Member to the Battle for the Seat

The Republic of China was a founding member of the United Nations and one of the five permanent members of the Security Council. On June 26, 1945, in San Francisco, T.V. Soong signed the UN Charter on behalf of the Republic of China; on August 24 of the same year, Chiang Kai-shek signed the instrument of ratification in Chongqing[^2].

The problem emerged after 1949: The People's Republic of China was established in Beijing, and the Republic of China government retreated to Taiwan, but both sides claimed to be the sole legitimate representatives of "China." Starting in the 1950s, every UN General Assembly had to argue over "China's representation." In 1961, the United States proposed the "Important Issues Resolution," defining "changing China's representation" as an "important issue" requiring a two-thirds majority to pass, effectively blocking Beijing[^3].

Former Foreign Minister E.C.Y. Yu had a famous metaphor: "The 'One China' policy is like painting the floor of a room—once you finish painting, you step out of the room."[^4] This prophecy came true in 1970. At the 25th UN General Assembly, the "Expel Us, Admit the Bandits" proposal proposed by Albania won a simple majority for the first time (51 to 49). The Republic of China retained its seat, relying only on the two-thirds threshold of the "Important Issues Resolution" to block it[^3].

The red light was on.

## Chiang Kai-shek's Hesitation: Two Chinas in the Diary

In the past, academia often blamed Chiang Kai-shek's stubbornness of "neither Han nor bandit can coexist" for the exit from the UN. However, after the National Palace Museum opened the *Diaries of Chiang Chung-cheng* and diplomatic archives in 2010, the historical image was rewritten.

Scholars studying these archives, such as Wang Hao, point out that on December 16, 1970, Chiang Kai-shek explicitly instructed the Ministry of Foreign Affairs at the National Security Council to study various UN regulations, "to do our utmost to maintain our legal status in the UN, but also to prepare for the worst-case scenario of exiting the UN"[^3]. In a diplomatic meeting on December 31, he told the group of ambassadors more clearly that he did not oppose studying the "dual representation" plan, but insisted on retaining the Security Council permanent member seat[^3].

In other words, "neither Han nor bandit can coexist" was a strategy to maintain legal legitimacy domestically and negotiate with the U.S.; privately, Chiang Kai-shek had already psychologically accepted "two Chinas." However, his red line was the Security Council seat—this represented the symbolic legal legitimacy of the Republic of China as a "great power."

On May 26, 1971, Vice President Yen Chia-kan presided over the National Security Council on behalf of Chiang. Secretary-General of the National Security Council Huang Shao-gu and Foreign Minister Chou Shu-kai proposed a surprising shift in plan: if a diplomatic ally wanted to establish relations with Beijing, as long as three premises were met (not recognizing the CCP as the sole legitimate government of China, not involving the Republic of China's territory Taiwan, and continuing to recognize our government as the Government of the Republic of China, not the "Taiwan Government"), diplomatic relations could be maintained[^3]. This was essentially accepting the arrangement of "two Chinas." Chiang Ching-kuo expressed on the spot that he "needed to think about it further."

Chiang's diary also reveals his inner torment. He wrote, "The ancient teaching of being the pillar in midstream and fighting alone as a lone army is a glorious isolation," but a few days later changed it to "weak country diplomacy cannot lack internal firmness and external flexibility; there should be a firm decision in mind, but the external form has not reached the time for implementation, and should adapt to the environment and change accordingly"[^3]. On May 27, he was diagnosed with cardiomegaly, and officially fell ill in mid-June, recovering only by the end of July—this window of absence coincided exactly with the timing of Kissinger's secret visit to Beijing.

## Dual Representation: The Path Not Taken

In early 1971, the Nixon administration formally proposed the "Dual Representation Plan"—allowing the People's Republic of China to take the Security Council permanent seat while retaining the Republic of China's membership in the General Assembly[^5]. On April 23, Nixon sent special envoy Robert Murphy to meet Chiang Kai-shek. To get Chiang's nod, Murphy unilaterally promised "that the Republic of China would retain its Security Council seat"—this clearly exceeded the White House's directives and authorization[^3].

Chiang Kai-shek finally relented. But he warned Murphy: if the Security Council seat was stripped, he "had no choice but rather die as a broken jade than live as a whole tile."

The problem was that Murphy had no authority to make this promise. On May 27, Nixon told Kissinger and Secretary of State William Rogers the truth: "I really wish we had failed by sticking to principles, so they could get out." Kissinger more sinisterly suggested: another way to "be defeated" is to delay and not decide, finally pretending to take a "two Chinas" stance and failing—"that also means we did everything we could."[^3]

This delaying strategy ultimately succeeded, "fooling Rogers and the U.S. Ambassador to the UN Bush (the future President George H.W. Bush), as well as Taiwan's diplomats, and even many historians in later generations"[^3]. George H.W. Bush was a sincere proponent of the dual representation plan that year, desperately pulling votes in the General Assembly hall until the last moment—he did not know he was being used as a gun[^6].

## Kissinger's July: The Backstab

On the afternoon of July 1, 1971, before Kissinger's secret visit to Beijing, he spoke with Shen Chien-hung, the Republic of China's Ambassador to the United States. Kissinger solemnly said: "President Nixon has deep friendship for our President and our government... His determination to do his best to maintain our seat in the General Assembly is beyond doubt. President Nixon also absolutely will not consider any measures that would cause us to lose our Security Council seat, please rest assured."[^3]

Ten days later, what Kissinger said to Zhou Enlai in Beijing was completely opposite.

From July 9 to 11, pretending to have a stomachache in Pakistan, Kissinger secretly flew to Beijing to meet Zhou Enlai. According to later declassified dialogue records[^7], Kissinger voluntarily revealed the bottom line of the U.S.'s "complex dual representation" plan to Zhou Enlai: Beijing could join the UN with a simple majority and take the Security Council seat, but expelling the Republic of China required a two-thirds majority. He promised Zhou Enlai: "As long as China joins the UN, then obtaining a two-thirds vote to expel Taiwan is only a matter of time; the key lies in both China and the U.S. knowing this arrangement in their hearts, reaching a tacit understanding, and waiting patiently."[^3]

Zhou Enlai was not pleased: "We have already waited for 21 years." And warned: "Taiwan will also oppose it, and the voices of opposition will come from all directions." Kissinger raised his hand in surrender, admitting this "is a very good way to end this issue"[^3].

On July 15, Nixon publicly announced Kissinger's visit to China. Small and medium-sized countries that were still观望 (waiting and seeing) began to change their stances. From October 20 to 26, when the General Assembly was debating China's representation, where was Kissinger? Again in Beijing—this time a public visit, paving the way for Nixon's visit to China the following year[^8]. Taipei diplomats described: "We are fighting bloody battles on the front line, while allies are drinking tea in the enemy's capital."

Former Deputy Minister of Foreign Affairs Chien Fu stated directly in a later interview: "The U.S. preferred to sacrifice the Republic of China to woo the CCP."[^9]

## Those 17 Minutes: Chou Shu-kai Steps Down from the Podium

The agenda on October 25 was very dramatic.

In the afternoon, Saudi Arabian representative Jamil Baroody proposed a compromise plan: allowing Beijing to take the Security Council seat, and Taiwan to remain in the General Assembly under the name "Republic of Taiwan," waiting for the two sides to resolve differences themselves[^7]. If this proposal had passed, Taiwan's fate might have been completely rewritten. But the Republic of China delegation insisted on "One China" and did not actively strive for it; the proposal was shelved.

At 9:47 PM, the vote on the "Important Issues Resolution"—55 to 59, rejected. At this moment, the battle was decided.

Chou Shu-kai immediately raised his hand to speak. He stepped onto the podium and read a pre-prepared statement: "The Republic of China has decided to withdraw from the United Nations which it participated in creating." He then led Liu Kai (then Permanent Representative to the UN) and other delegation members out of the hall[^1][^10].

At 10:25 PM, Resolution 2758 was put to a vote: 76 in favor, 35 against, 17 abstentions, 3 absent. The resolution text had only four short paragraphs. The key sentence was: "Decides to restore all the rights of the People's Republic of China... and immediately expels the representatives of Chiang Kai-shek from the seat they illegally occupy in the United Nations Organization and in all its specialized agencies."[^11]

Notably: The resolution text does not contain the word "Taiwan," nor does it define the territorial scope of "China"[^11]. This became the spark for 50 years of international legal disputes.

## Chiang Kai-shek's "Message to Compatriots Nationwide" and Domestic Impact

Early on the morning of October 26, Chiang Kai-shek issued the "Message to Compatriots Nationwide on the Republic of China's Exit from the United Nations": "Our country's stance of 'neither Han nor bandit can coexist' and the dignity of upholding the Charter... forces us to painfully announce our withdrawal from the United Nations organization which we participated in creating with difficulty."[^12] The eight characters at the end, "Revere Yourself and Strengthen Yourself, Remain Calm in the Face of Change," were printed in textbooks and carved on school gates for the next 30 years.

But the domestic impact was far more cruel than the official language. Alumni of the National Taiwan University Law School, Hung San-hsiung and Chen Ling-yu, recalled: "When the news reached the mainland, the dream of 'counter-attack the mainland and liberate compatriots' was shattered in an instant, let alone 'neither Han nor bandit can coexist' becoming empty talk."[^13] This defeat directly ignited the NTU student movement and the "Protect the Diaoyu Islands Movement" between 1971 and 1973. Students began to question the party-state myth, and many future leaders of the non-party and democratic movements were politically enlightened in this storm.

In his 2021 book *1971 Mythical United Nations*, Yao Chia-wen stated directly that Chiang Kai-shek "preferred to be expelled rather than represent Taiwan"—if the Balodis proposal had been accepted and Taiwan had stayed under the name "Taiwan," Taiwan's international situation afterwards would have been completely different[^14]. But Wang Hao's research points out: the problem actually lay in Nixon and Kissinger having already decided to sacrifice Taiwan; Chiang Kai-shek "was too flexible to save it"—this is a story of two national leaders joining hands to eliminate an ally[^3].

## Echoes 50 Years Later: What Exactly Does Resolution 2758 Say?

The consequences of exiting the UN were domino-like. From 1971 to 1979, the number of countries breaking diplomatic relations with the Republic of China dropped sharply from 68 to only 22; Japan broke relations in 1972, and the U.S. in 1979[^15]. Taiwan was excluded from WHO, ICAO, and Interpol, and even passports were often misrecognized.

But since the 2020s, the international community has begun to re-examine Resolution 2758.

Starting in 2024, the U.S., UK, European Parliament, Australia, Netherlands, and other countries successively passed resolutions or statements, explicitly pointing out: Resolution 2758 only addressed "China's representation," did not address "Taiwan's political status," and did not authorize China to represent Taiwan[^16]. On April 23, 2025, the U.S. publicly criticized China for "distorting" Resolution 2758 for the first time at the UN Security Council; on May 5, 2025, the U.S. House of Representatives passed the *Taiwan International Solidarity Act* without dissent, formally confirming this stance in the form of domestic law[^17].

Returning to those 17 minutes in 1971—the steps Chou Shu-kai took down from the podium locked Taiwan's international situation for half a century. But the text of the resolution itself did not completely close the door. The gray area left between the cracks is precisely the space that Taiwan's diplomats are striving to expand today.
