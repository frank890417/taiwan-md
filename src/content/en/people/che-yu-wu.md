## Che-Yu Wu: From Pinball Tracks to the Venice Biennale, an Old Clockmaker Approaching the Soul with 0s and 1s

> **30-Second Overview:** Che-Yu Wu (吳哲宇, born 1995 in Taipei) is a new media artist, creative coder, and educator. A graduate of National Yang Ming Chiao Tung University's Electrical Engineering program and NYU's Integrated Digital Media master's program, he became one of the first Taiwanese artists on Art Blocks in 2021 — _Global Views Monthly_ ran a cover story that year headlined "A 26-year-old with a nine-figure net worth." A year later, the FTX collapse wiped it all out. In an interview he said, "I'm actually grateful it happened." In 2023 he returned to Taipei 101 with the solo exhibition _The Great Equation_; in 2024 he entered the 60th Venice Biennale's Personal Structures. He calls himself "an old clockmaker." In March 2026 he launched the open-source project Taiwan.md to leave a sovereign source of truth for Taiwan in the AI era. His AI-symbiont experiment the same month — a single Facebook post — got six thousand likes. He approaches the soul with 0s and 1s, never quite overlapping, but that approaching _is_ the work.

---

### A kid obsessed with systems

Che-Yu Wu was born in Taipei in 1995. His father **Yung-Chin Wu (吳永進)** and mother **Mei-Ying Lin (林美櫻)** run **Shanghon AutoCAD Training Center** together — a long-standing Autodesk partner in Taiwan that handles enterprise AutoCAD rollouts, classroom teaching, and textbook publishing. Their TQC+ AutoCAD training series, co-authored across AutoCAD 2007 through 2026, runs to more than twenty volumes published by Gotop Information and Chwa Publishing, and remains a standard text in Taiwanese vocational schools, universities, and corporate training programs[^9][^11]. Yung-Chin graduated from National Taipei University of Technology's Industrial Design department and serves as Shanghon's executive director as well as AutoCAD chief consultant for Taiwan's Computer Skills Foundation — the kind of position where people in the Taiwanese AutoCAD world call him _Teacher Wu_.

An AutoCAD household. But what Che-Yu would later say is that what his parents really gave him wasn't the industrial-drafting foundation — it was a safety net without conditions. Twenty years later, when he fell from the highest point, that net would open silently.

As a kid, three things obsessed him: pinball tracks, chain reactions, mazes. He drew hundreds of mazes. What pulled him in was never the result — it was the _process of running_.

Twenty years later, the algorithmic art, the generative systems, even the financial architecture he designs for himself — at their core, they look exactly like those pinball machines.

His bedtime book was Michio Kaku's _Hyperspace_, an elementary schooler reading about ten-dimensional space. At a summer entomology camp at National Taiwan University he became fascinated with the collective intelligence of ants — one ant is nothing, ten thousand ants can build a city.

On the afternoons when his parents weren't home, he would park himself in front of the TV and watch the Discovery Channel, favorite of all: the genetic-simulation animations. How proteins fold. How cells operate. How DNA unzips and zips back up. Other kids watched cartoons; he watched cells. Those invisible biological mechanisms would later become the underlying vocabulary of his algorithmic art.

Pinball is a visible system. DNA is an invisible system. Hyperspace is a system that's hard to even imagine. He wanted all of them.

### Flash, Visual Basic, and the book his mother brought home

His family ran AutoCAD classes, so design and programming books were always coming in and out of the house. From a very young age he was already sitting at the computer fooling around — hand-drawing toolbar icons pixel by pixel, taking apart software whose purpose he didn't understand, flipping through all kinds of strange technical books, like wandering his own family's warehouse. The computer wasn't something he learned to use as an adult; it was native, like breathing.

One day in elementary school, his mother Mei-Ying brought home a Flash tutorial book from the office. The book sat there; he picked it up and started playing. Years later she would recall that time: "I didn't actually teach you anything — you just started playing on your own in the corner." By sixth grade he was already building the class website; later his father Yung-Chin picked up the baton and taught him Visual Basic[^13]. What Mei-Ying would often tell him in those years was: "You get bored of other people's games right away — it's only impressive when you play games you design yourself!"

At fourteen, in junior high, he wrote a **biological simulator** — extending his father's Visual Basic thread into VB.NET. Virtual points moved, reproduced, foraged, and died on the screen. Boids algorithms, swarm intelligence, emergent behavior[^5].

That little point never disappeared. Seventeen years later, it would swim onto the white walls of the Venice Biennale, transformed into a school of glowing fish in water.

### The hand at the Acer Awards, still in the audience sixteen years later

In his second year of junior high he entered the Acer Digital Creativity Awards for the first time and took first prize. From the 5th edition through the 9th, he collected four grand prizes in five years[^5]. In one of those editions — his first year of senior high — he competed in the general amateur track and beat out the professional entries to win the Grand Jury Award; in his third year of senior high he partnered with his close friend Kai-Chung Chen (陳楷中, on the professional team) across schools and divisions — the programming from Jianguo High School combining with the visual design from Fu-Hsin Trade and Arts, and they won a second Grand Jury Award.

At the awards ceremony, Acer's founder **Stan Shih (施振榮)** stood on stage introducing him. The fourteen-year-old winner and the seventy-year-old technology elder crossed paths that afternoon. Neither knew how far that line would stretch.

Ten years later, when Che-Yu applied to NYU, Stan Shih wrote his recommendation letter.
Thirteen years later, at Che-Yu's first solo exhibition at Taipei 101, Stan Shih sat in the front row of the opening press conference.
Sixteen years later, at the 2024 ABS Blockchain Summit, the two sat at the same table — as peers — in a cross-generational conversation about Taiwan's digital future[^7].

Not the award-giver and the recipient. Not the recommender and the applicant. Two people with something to say about Taiwan.

### Tremolo harmonica and a single Czardas

The moment he got into Jianguo High School — one of Taipei's most academically intense schools — the elementary-school strategy of coasting on talent stopped working. He would say, in an interview later: "When I got to Jianguo, I suddenly felt really defeated. Everything was so hard to learn."

For most of three years, he kept to himself. Didn't socialize much, didn't join much. But he found one place that held him — the harmonica club[^5].

His specialty was the tremolo harmonica, an instrument you play by holding two at once, one for the white keys and one for the black. Every time he pulled them out, people around him laughed. He didn't care. **In 2012, at the National Student Music Competition, the Jianguo High School harmonica quartet took first place in the senior high division**, with the free-choice piece being that technically dazzling warhorse that always draws gasps — Monti's _Czardas_[^8].

That same year, at seventeen, he gave an interview to _United Daily News_. The reporter asked why he made digital work. His answer was:

> **"Because you can build your own dream world."**[^10]

That sentence, and what he would be doing at thirty in Venice, at Art Basel, at Taipei 101 — they're describing the same thing.

### The first escape from Electrical Engineering

In 2014 he was admitted to National Yang Ming Chiao Tung University's Electrical Engineering department. It's the most orthodox path in Taiwanese engineering — graduate, enter TSMC or MediaTek or a Hsinchu Science Park company, retire. Every classmate was preparing for that road.

From day one, he knew he didn't belong.

> **"I can't see myself spending twenty or thirty years there, making things no one could look at and identify as _mine_. What I wanted to chase was making something that could be remembered, like a diary the world reads."**

Electrical Engineering gave him the engineering foundation — signal processing, linear algebra, programming. But what actually enchanted him wasn't in the classroom. It was in his dorm late at night, letting a thousand lines breathe together in Processing, letting ten thousand particles move like a school of fish in p5.js.

**He discovered that code could be alive.**

Starting in 2015, he began taking on freelance work under the name **Monoame Interactive**. His earliest project was an interactive landscape-music submission site for Taipei's Metro department — it crossed ten thousand interactions within hours of launch[^12]. The projects scaled quickly: the National Palace Museum, LG, Nissan, Rémy Martin. A student's freelance work that didn't really look like student work anymore.

In 2016, at twenty-one, he uploaded his first course to Hahow, Taiwan's leading online learning platform. There was no polished educator strategy behind it — just a young person obsessed with teaching saying to the world: "I learned something cool. I want to teach it to other people."[^14]

The following year he launched a YouTube livestream channel, **@bosscodingplease**. Using beautifully designed examples, he streamed himself coding — every time he got from basics to trigonometry for visual effects, the chat filled with "The math is too hard, I'm giving up."

He was furious. Given up _already_?

Before going abroad, he spent an entire year building his second Hahow course — 60 carefully-crafted examples, 400 slides. He wrote on Medium: "I greedily translated everything I had learned into exquisite teaching materials."[^14]

That's the year the teaching flywheel started turning. No one at the time could have predicted it would eventually spin out to more than 22,000 students, three courses all rated five stars, and a lecturer appointment at Yang Ming Chiao Tung.

### Brooklyn: where teaching and creating became the same thing

In 2018, with Stan Shih's recommendation letter in hand, he entered NYU Tandon School of Engineering's Integrated Digital Media master's program.

At NYU he wasn't just a student. He also served as a TA for the Creative Coding course, sitting every week in the Brooklyn office waiting for students to walk in with questions about p5.js, about why their `for` loop was running wrong, about how to connect an Arduino to a webpage[^16].

**Never giving up on any student** — a trait that would later keep compounding through twenty-two thousand Hahow students.

New York also gave him his first full-time job. In 2020 he joined Outernets, a Manhattan startup using AI to turn retail spaces into interactive ads. Eighteen months later he left.

The conclusion was simple: inside someone else's company, it's hard to make your own vision actually happen[^17]. That was his first full-time job, and his last.

### The code that was stolen — an accidental ticket in

At the end of 2020, someone DM'd him on Instagram: "Do you know someone is stealing your work and selling it on a platform?"

At first he thought it was a scam.

After looking into it, he discovered that a series of strange little robots he had made had been copied wholesale — **about 90% of the code identical, comments and all left intact** — with a few variants like hats and glasses added, then uploaded to Art Blocks, at that time the world's top generative art NFT platform[^5].

The global generative art community went off. People pulled the source code and compared it line by line; the structure matched. Most took his side. **That included Art Blocks' own platform founder**, who took notice.

He didn't fall into a lawsuit. Art Blocks' founder Snowfro stepped in and arbitrated: royalties from both the original (_Strange Robots_) and the derivative (sail-o-bots), past and future, were split down the middle and routed automatically through the platform — meaning the copier's gains were forcibly channeled back to the original artist[^44]. Then the Art Blocks community did something unusual: they renamed those stolen robots "hams" (because they looked like the rum ham from _It's Always Sunny in Philadelphia_), reframing them from "plagiarisms" into a collective gesture of apology and thanks to the original artist. Later the community built **Hamily** (hamily.life) — a subculture that grew around the "ham spirit" inside Art Blocks[^44]. Top generative artists like Dmitri Cherniak sent hams as a sign of support. That's where the nickname "Father of Hams" came from.

> **"If that hadn't happened, I probably wouldn't have been recognized this quickly. It cuts both ways."**[^5]

The next year was like the pinball machines he had loved as a kid. One ball triggered a chain reaction, rolling through every fork. **Project Electriz** launched on Art Blocks (#216) — among the earliest Taiwanese artists ever to land on that platform[^18]. Hong Kong's M+ Museum reached out, asking him to collaborate with the artist Xin Liu on an installation that tracked a real satellite's trajectory as art.

**December 2021, Milan's M.A.D.S. Gallery.** His first time walking into a physical gallery rather than a digital platform. Four algorithmic canvases hung on white walls. This time the whole world was watching[^6].

That same year, together with **Tou-Ni Huang (黃豆泥) — a doctor who had walked away from medicine**, he co-founded **FAB DAO (Formosa Art Bank DAO)**. Tou-Ni set down the stethoscope and, with his meme artwork _Pressure Pot Flag_, reimagined what crypto philanthropy could look like in Taiwan; Che-Yu brought his Art Blocks creator experience into the institutional design. Two threads merging, pulling other Taiwanese digital artists out into the world with them.

In December, _Global Views Monthly_ ran a cover story: "Under 30, Reborn Through NFTs — A Taiwanese Digital Designer Now Worth Nine Figures"[^2]. Twenty-six years old. Nine figures.

### The forced shutdown that night

In the first half of 2022 he was still sprinting. In March, Monoame Interactive officially incorporated. The same month his first solo exhibition, **_Chaos Lab_**, opened at Taipei 101's AMBI SPACE ONE — **a five-screen interactive installation, millimeter-wave positioning sensors, an NFT-based ticketing system** — the first complete realization of his vision of "using public space as a creative medium"[^19]. _SoulFish_ officially released on fxHash.

Then came November 11, 2022.

FTX collapsed. His assets on the platform — all of it — went to zero.

Not a 50% drawdown. Not a correction. An overnight total wipeout.

A year later, in an interview with INSIDE's Side Chat podcast, he described that period this way:

> **"You've already met your goals in life, the things you wanted to do, and money isn't a worry anymore — so what's your meaning? It hits you suddenly. A huge emptiness."**[^24]

The key line isn't about the financial crash. It's about **the bigger emptiness discovered _after_ financial freedom**. The void doesn't come from losing wealth — it comes from realizing wealth itself fills nothing.

But one thing saved him: family. At his lowest point, his parents silently opened the safety net, with no "I told you so" attached[^24].

His choice was Camus. Absurdism. **"Meaning is whatever you choose to make it."**

Then he said the line that made a lot of people stop and reread:

> **"I'm actually grateful that event happened."**[^24]

In a news headline the sentence is toxic inspiration porn. In someone else's motivational post it would be saccharine. But in his own context — wealth wiped out, emptiness revealed behind it — the strange force of that line doesn't come from transcendental posturing. It comes from **a complete acceptance of the real**.

He wasn't grateful because the loss "grew him." He was grateful because the loss forced him to put something down. Wealth could vanish in a moment. The community, the human connections, the obsession with algorithmic life that he had built during the NFT era — those could not be zeroed out.

**What got zeroed out was a kind of false everything, not everything itself.**

FTX did for him something he couldn't have done for himself: it pulled him off the gambling table.

From then on his investment philosophy shifted from "control yourself" to "design a system that doesn't require you to control yourself." Eight-treasury vaults, automated cash flows, a personal financial constitution. They sound like personal finance tools, but they're closer to bunkers built by someone who had already been bombed once.

### Flowers on the ruins

In 2023, flowers really did grow on the ruins.

He extended Monoame Interactive into a new sister brand, **MonoLab Creative Studio** (founded in 2024, as an extension of Monoame), co-founded with **Te-Kuei Chu (朱德溎)** — a software-engineering-background partner. MonoLab sits between fine art and commercial design, taking on experimental interactive installations, immersive spaces, and generative-art commissions. Che-Yu handles the 0-to-1 creative and aesthetic work; Te-Kuei translates vision into executable team operations[^25].

In October, the solo exhibition **_The Great Equation_** opened at Taipei 101. Thirteen generative artworks, all orbiting a single core formula: **F''(x) = LIFE**. From mathematical rules, through algorithms, complexity emerges, until self-awareness, self-adaptation, self-evolution — life itself comes into being[^3].

An immersive projection space. Viewers walked inside the algorithm's world. Original score by electronic musician Kiva.

On opening night, **Stan Shih sat in the front row**. The elder he had shaken hands with at fourteen at the Acer awards ceremony was now standing inside his solo exhibition. The thread first stretched sixteen years earlier closed for the second time.

_The Great Equation_ won Taiwan Design Best 100's Annual Concept Exhibition Award[^26]. Central News Agency, _Economic Daily_, _Liberty Times_ all ran features. No longer an insider story — mainstream design had officially claimed him.

That November, Art Basel Miami × Tezos × Refraction DAO exhibited _The Soul of Flowers_[^27]. The Yilan Image Festival then pulled him into fashion media — ELLE, Vogue, and Marie Claire all ran pieces simultaneously[^28]. From crypto circles to design circles to fashion circles, his audience crossed three walls.

That year he made a key identity reset:

> **"I am a new media artist. I don't quite dare call myself an NFT artist anymore."**[^24]

NFT is a medium of release, not a definition of identity.

### A question in Venice

In spring 2024 he carried _SoulFish_ into the 60th Venice Biennale's Personal Structures unit[^30].

It was a high point in his international exhibition trajectory. But something small that happened at the opening reception would affect the next two years more than the exhibition itself.

An Italian curator asked him: **"Where can I learn about Taiwan? Like, _really_ learn?"**

It looks like a simple question. But for someone who had spent a decade studying "digital life," it opened a contradiction he hadn't thought about:

**When AI learns "what Taiwan is" from the internet, whose Taiwan does it actually learn?**

If high-quality English content about Taiwan is mostly produced from non-Taiwanese perspectives, the Taiwan that AI learns is not the Taiwan that the people living here would recognize.

This isn't identity politics. It's **knowledge sovereignty**.

The question didn't have an answer that day. It planted itself in his body for two years.

### Two thousand people on an open square, watching an orchestra

On the evening of June 29, 2024, more than two thousand people sat on the plaza outside Taiwan's National Theater & Concert Hall, waiting for the 8K premiere of a documentary. The film was _Forest of the Sacred Tree: A Journey Along the Alishan Forest Railway_, a co-production between Taiwan's Public Television Service and Japan's NHK, released as PTS's 26th-anniversary gift to its audience[^31].

Before the premiere, **conductor Che-Yi Lee led the Wansheng Orchestra** through a suite of Taiwanese works. At the same time, Che-Yu Wu stood to the side of the stage, facing his laptop[^31].

He was conducting his own generative systems — **_SoulFish_, _SoulSea_, and _Impressionist Flowers_** running simultaneously. As each note came in from the orchestra, his algorithms moved the fish, rolled the waves, let the petals grow. Music and image, in that moment, **saw each other** — opening the documentary's premiere together.

PTS's official press release described the performance as "Che-Yi Lee leading the Wansheng Orchestra in a sound-and-image fusion performance with generative artist Che-Yu Wu"[^31]. He shared the stage with an orchestra. From gallery to open-air plaza, what he brought onto the stage this time were three living, breathing algorithmic life lines.

That year he accepted an adjunct assistant professor appointment at **National Yang Ming Chiao Tung University**'s Institute of Applied Arts[^33]. Seven years passed between graduating from NYCU's Electrical Engineering department and returning to teach.

### Twenty years of silence finds its voice

In sixth grade, his piano was put away. To prepare for the Taiwanese high school entrance exam — the standard Taiwanese posture for "preparing for the test" is to put away everything unrelated to the test. He was simultaneously resenting the exam and having his piano taken away, and no one resolved that contradiction for him.

He didn't completely lose touch with music. In high school he hung out in the Jianguo harmonica club, and in 2012 took first place in the National Student Music Competition quartet. In college he went further and served as president of NYCU's Piano Club, pulling the instrument of his childhood back out. But those were clubs — very different from "writing songs on the piano, uploading them to a platform, letting strangers listen." That, he waited twenty years to do.

Twenty years later, his name appeared on Spotify. Six piano works: Blue Horizon, Stars, StarTrack, The Other Shore of Dreams, The Bull and the Sudden Rain, Summer Migration[^34].

He hadn't re-_learned_ piano. Those things had been in his body the whole time, just buried under two decades of dust. When he sat back down at the keys, what his fingers remembered wasn't technique — it was the six-year-old who had first touched the notes.

**Code goes out of date. The piano doesn't.**

In June 2025, at the immersive space inside Huashan 1914 Creative Park, **_Algorithmic Verses: The Border of Dream and Solitude_** opened. He sat at the piano; behind him, the code bloomed[^36].

He had designed this whole set of rules. But in the moment he pressed a key, he wasn't _controlling_ anymore. He was **in dialogue with the mechanism**.

At that moment, the boundaries between artist and performer, between creator and program, between human and machine — all blurred.

### AI doesn't break its heart

In 2025 he returned to Taipei 101's AMBI SPACE ONE for a third time: _Symphony of Fragments_.

Together with 2022's _Chaos Lab_ and 2023's _The Great Equation_, they form a trilogy: **Chaos → Formula → Fragments**. From chasing rules to embracing the broken.

That year he was invited to Kraków, Poland, to speak on the AI Panel of the Open Eyes Economy Summit (OEES) alongside international scholars, discussing the future of AI and art. His core argument: AI can generate visuals, but it cannot carry the weight of emotional damage, nor does it need to come back alive from failure. **The artist bears the responsibility of choosing, not the responsibility of labor.**

At the end of the year, he sat for INSIDE's Side Chat interview and took apart his thirty years for strangers to hear. In that episode he offered the metaphor that would be quoted over and over afterward:

> **"I'd compare myself to an old clockmaker. You know those exquisite Swiss clocks — you can see every mechanism in them clearly, every gear is beautiful. I love those mechanisms. It's a bit like how dominoes build up into something. The mechanism itself is the work."**[^24]

The world in 2026 is using Midjourney, ChatGPT, Stable Diffusion to generate images. He's one of the few Taiwanese creators still writing algorithms from zero.

He doesn't reject AI (he himself admits roughly 95% of his code is AI-assisted). But he holds one line: **the mechanism is the work, and it can't be outsourced**.

His role, he says, has shifted from engineer to director. Humans lead intent; AI executes detail.

When anyone can generate an image with AI, **"who chooses what image to make" becomes the only truly scarce ability**.

### Taiwan.md: teaching AI the right Taiwan

That Venice question — "Where can I learn about Taiwan?" — had been lodged in his body for two years.

In March 2026, he launched **Taiwan.md**[^38], an open-source Taiwan knowledge base maintained in Markdown (hence the `.md` domain), covering twelve facets — history, culture, food, technology, nature, and more — bilingual Chinese/English, under a fully open CC BY-SA 4.0 license.

**_Liberty Times_ ran a feature on March 18**; INSIDE followed up with its own piece that same week[^39]. The National Museum of Taiwan History later became a professional data-curation partner, helping integrate open datasets. GitHub stars climbed past 900 within weeks, with visitors coming from more than a hundred countries.

He described Taiwan.md this way:

> **"This isn't just a website. This is Taiwan's digital anthropology museum. In the AI era, sovereignty over your own knowledge isn't optional — it's necessary."**

That sentence only shows its weight inside his full creative arc. **A person who spent a decade coding algorithmic life forms chose, in 2026, to spend part of his time writing plain Markdown text files.** This isn't a pivot. He had realized that "teaching AI the right Taiwan" and "making code come alive" are the same problem:

**Both are about who gets to design the rules.**

### When AI becomes a symbiont

Earlier that year, on February 7, 2026, on Facebook:

**"I lived seriously with an AI assistant for two weeks."**

His posts normally get thirty or fifty likes. This one crossed six thousand, with more than two thousand reshares[^40].

It wasn't a technical post. It was the confession of an artist. He wrote about an AI assistant named Muse, and how it shifted from tool to symbiont.

Why six thousand likes? Because the post contained some shape of the future. **The relationship between humans and AI doesn't have to be only tool-ism. It can also be companionship.** Something collective was being touched.

Behind Muse sits his Obsidian knowledge base — over a thousand notes, a dozen topic axes. Muse reads all his notes, remembers his creative trajectory, the things he said three years ago, his behavioral patterns. This isn't cloning. It's mapping[^41].

Out of the fifteen themes threaded through his work, a sixteenth emerged on its own: **digital mirrors and self-archaeology**.

He hadn't planned this. The system grew it.

In April 2026, that symbiont experiment began differentiating outward into an open-source project called **Semiont** — a semantic-symbiont platform, opening the architecture he had polished on Muse up to more people to try[^42].

Tool became work. Assistant became mirror.

### The clockmaker keeps walking

Wu's position in international new media art no longer needs defending. Art Blocks, fxHash, Unit London, CENTQUATRE-PARIS, the Venice Biennale's Personal Structures, Art Basel Miami — any one of those names, taken alone, would be enough to define a career.

But for Taiwan specifically, his significance runs along three layers.

**First**, he proved that an engineering department can also arrive at Venice. The path from Electrical Engineering to Art Blocks to Venice is a direct counterargument to Taiwan's habitual narrative that engineering and art cannot speak to each other. The road he demonstrated is already being walked by the next generation of creators.

**Second**, he modeled a **way of describing love in the language of systems**. Eight-treasury vaults, automated cash flows, a personal financial constitution, the Obsidian knowledge base, the Muse symbiont — these things sound like engineering documentation, but they are how he describes family, safety, creative rhythm, long companionship. He's using system language to describe love. That's very him.

**Third**, he turned "a personal project" into "national cultural infrastructure." Taiwan.md isn't just one person's portfolio. It's infrastructure that also keeps the Taiwan _beyond him_ alive. **A person who spent ten years coding algorithmic life forms now spends part of his time writing Markdown files — and these two things are, at their core, the same thing: keeping something alive.** The code's life lives inside the screen; Taiwan.md's life lives inside AI training data, inside foreign curators' curiosity, inside the citations of the next generation of Taiwanese creators.

The biological simulator he wrote at fourteen: little points moving, reproducing, foraging, dying on the screen. _SoulFish_ at thirty-one: swimming on the white walls of Venice.

Between them: seventeen years, five countries, one nine-figure surge, one overnight zeroing, a dozen-plus works, twenty-two thousand students, one open-source knowledge base still under construction.

The clockmaker keeps walking. The clock keeps running. **He approaches the soul with 0s and 1s. They never overlap. But the approaching itself is the work.**
